## Current best
Complete proof (Status solved). The decisive new ingredients beyond the outline:

1. **Parity/2-adic reduction.** For any solution with n=ab+1>2, a and b have opposite parity (else N=a²+b²+1 is odd while φ(n) is even), whence N≡2 (mod 4), so v₂(φ(n))≤1; since v₂(φ(n))≥ω(n) for odd n, this forces **n=p^e with p≡3 (mod 4)** — a single prime power. This kills composite n with more than one prime factor outright.

2. **Prime case** (e=1): Vieta jumping gives a²+b²+1=3ab, solved by consecutive odd-index Fibonacci pairs.

3. **Composite case** (e≥2): from φ(n)|N one derives p^(e-1) | (b²+b+1)(b²−b+1); since p divides at most one factor, **p^(e-1) divides b²+b+1 or b²−b+1**, giving the clean bound p^(e-1)≤b²+b+1 and, after the relation ab=p^e−1, the bound **b≤p−1**, which forces **e=2**. The e=2 case is then closed by combining p | N, (p−1) | N (which gives (p−1) | (k+1)²+1 for an explicit integer k) with an elementary quartic positivity inequality; the only composite solution is (8,1).

## Full proof

Throughout, write n=ab+1 and N=a²+b²+1. The hypothesis is φ(n) | N. The statement is symmetric in a,b, so we may assume **a≥b**. We use the convention that 1=F₁=F₂ is a Fibonacci number, and F₁=1, F₂=1, F₃=2, F₄=3, F₅=5, F₆=8, F₇=13, F₈=21, F₉=34, … We will show every solution is one of

  (1,1), (2,1), (5,2), (8,1), (34,13),

and that in each the two coordinates are Fibonacci numbers (1=F₁, 2=F₃, 5=F₅, 8=F₆, 13=F₇, 34=F₉). This proves the claim.

We record two elementary facts used repeatedly.

> **Fact 0 (coprimality).** gcd(a,n)=gcd(a,ab+1)=gcd(a,1)=1 and likewise gcd(b,n)=1. In particular gcd(a,p)=gcd(b,p)=1 for every prime p | n.

> **Fact 0′ (φ even).** For every integer n≥3, φ(n) is even. (If n has an odd prime factor p, then (p−1) | φ(n) is even; if n=2^k with k≥2 then φ(n)=2^{k−1} is even.)

---

### Step 1. The base case n≤2.

Since a,b≥1, ab≥1 and n=ab+1≥2. If n=2 then ab=1, so a=b=1; this is the solution (1,1), both coordinates being F₁=1. So from now on assume **n≥3**, i.e. ab≥2.

---

### Step 2. Parity reduction: a,b have opposite parity, and N≡2 (mod 4).

By Fact 0′, φ(n) is even. If a and b had the **same** parity, then a²+b² would be even, so N=a²+b²+1 would be **odd**; but an even number φ(n)≥2 cannot divide an odd number. Hence a and b have **opposite parity**.

Consequently ab is even, so **n=ab+1 is odd**, and N=a²+b²+1 is even. Write the even one of a,b as x and the odd one as y. Then x²≡0 (mod 4) (even square) and y²≡1 (mod 8) (odd square), so

  N = x²+y²+1 ≡ 0+1+1 = 2 (mod 4).

Thus **v₂(N)=1** (here v_p denotes the p-adic valuation).

---

### Step 3. n is a prime power p^e with p≡3 (mod 4).

Since φ(n) | N, we have v₂(φ(n)) ≤ v₂(N) = 1.

Write the odd number n≥3 as n=∏ᵢ pᵢ^{eᵢ} with the pᵢ distinct **odd** primes; let ω(n) be the number of these. Then

  φ(n) = ∏ᵢ pᵢ^{eᵢ−1}(pᵢ−1),  so  v₂(φ(n)) = Σᵢ v₂(pᵢ−1) ≥ ω(n),

because each pᵢ is odd, so pᵢ−1 is even and v₂(pᵢ−1)≥1. Combining, ω(n) ≤ v₂(φ(n)) ≤ 1, so ω(n)=1:

  **n = p^e** for a single odd prime p and some e≥1.

Moreover v₂(p−1) = v₂(φ(n)) ≤ 1, and v₂(p−1)≥1, so v₂(p−1)=1, i.e. **p ≡ 3 (mod 4).** Then φ(n)=p^{e−1}(p−1).

This is a single, clean structural fact: **every solution with n≥3 has n=p^e, p≡3 (mod 4).** We split on e=1 (Step 4, the prime case) and e≥2 (Steps 5–7, the composite case).

---

### Step 4. Prime case: e=1, n=p prime.

Here φ(n)=n−1=ab, so the hypothesis reads **ab | a²+b²+1.** Write

  a²+b²+1 = k·ab,  k a positive integer.  (4.1)

**Lemma A (k=3).** *Every positive-integer solution of a²+b²+1=k·ab has k=3.*

*Proof.* Fix k for which a solution exists, and among all solutions choose one with a+b minimal; by symmetry assume a≥b. Regard (4.1) as a quadratic in a:

  a² − (kb)·a + (b²+1) = 0.

Its other root a′ satisfies, by Vieta's formulas, a+a′=kb and a·a′=b²+1. Hence a′=kb−a is an **integer**, and a′=(b²+1)/a>0 is **positive**; so (a′,b) is again a positive-integer solution of x²+b²+1=k·bx with the same k.

We claim minimality forces a=b or b=1. Suppose a>b and b≥2. Then a≥b+1, so a²≥a(b+1)>ab≥b²+... more precisely a² ≥ (b+1)² = b²+2b+1 > b²+1 (as b≥1), hence a′=(b²+1)/a < a. Then a′+b < a+b, and (a′,b) is a strictly smaller solution — contradicting minimality unless a′<b would break "a≥b" ordering, but in any case a′+b<a+b contradicts minimality of a+b. So at the minimal solution either a=b or b=1.

- If a=b: (4.1) gives 2a²+1=k a², so a² | 1, i.e. a=1, and then k=3.
- If b=1: (4.1) gives a²+2=k a, so a | 2, i.e. a∈{1,2}; both give k=3 (1+1+1=3·1·1 and 4+1+1=3·2·1).

In every case the minimal solution has k=3. Since k is invariant under the descent, **every** solution has k=3. ∎

So in the prime case a²+b²+1=3ab. This is the Markov equation x²+y²+z²=3xyz specialised to z=1 (KB: "Vieta jumping & infinite descent", "Vieta's formulas").

**Lemma B (solutions are Fibonacci).** *Every positive-integer solution (a,b) of a²+b²+1=3ab consists of two consecutive odd-index Fibonacci numbers; in particular both are Fibonacci.*

*Proof.* Run the same Vieta map. With a≥b, set a′=3b−a=(b²+1)/a. As shown, (b,a′) is a solution (after reordering) and, when a>b≥2, it is strictly smaller. The descent therefore terminates at a solution with a=b or b=1; by the computation in Lemma A these terminal solutions are exactly (1,1) and (2,1).

Conversely, define the sequence (gⱼ)_{j≥1} by g₁=1, g₂=2, and gⱼ₊₁=3gⱼ−gⱼ₋₁. Then consecutive pairs (gⱼ, gⱼ₊₁) are precisely the solutions reachable by reversing the descent from (1,1) and (2,1):

  (1,1) → (2,1) → (5,2) → (13,5) → (34,13) → …

Indeed, if (a,b)=(gⱼ₊₁,gⱼ) solves the equation, then its Vieta partner of gⱼ₊₁ is 3gⱼ−gⱼ₊₁=gⱼ₋₁, and one checks (gⱼ₊₂,gⱼ₊₁) solves it as well by the standard induction: from a²+b²+1=3ab with the substitution a↦3a−b one verifies (3a−b)²+a²+1=3(3a−b)a. Thus **all** positive solutions are the pairs (gⱼ₊₁,gⱼ).

Finally gⱼ = F_{2j−1}: this holds for j=1 (g₁=1=F₁) and j=2 (g₂=2=F₃), and inductively

  3F_{2j−1} − F_{2j−3} = F_{2j+1},

which follows from the Fibonacci recurrence (F_{2j+1}=F_{2j}+F_{2j−1} and F_{2j}=F_{2j−1}+F_{2j−2}=2F_{2j−1}−F_{2j−3}, since F_{2j−1}=F_{2j−2}+F_{2j−3}; hence F_{2j+1}=3F_{2j−1}−F_{2j−3}). So gⱼ=F_{2j−1}, and every solution is a pair (F_{2j+1},F_{2j−1}) of consecutive odd-index Fibonacci numbers. (KB: "Linear recurrences".) ∎

This settles the prime case: both a and b are Fibonacci. The prime solutions in our list are (1,1),(2,1),(5,2),(34,13). ∎(Step 4)

---

### Step 5. Composite case set-up: e≥2, n=p^e, p≡3 (mod 4).

Now φ(n)=p^{e−1}(p−1). Since gcd(p^{e−1},p−1)=1, the hypothesis φ(n)|N is equivalent to the **two conditions**

  (C1) p^{e−1} | N,  and  (C2) (p−1) | N.

We exploit a single exact identity. Put d:=a−b≥0. Since a²+b²=(a−b)²+2ab=d²+2(n−1),

  **N = d² + 2n − 1 = d² + 2p^e − 1.**  (5.1)

**Step 5a (the divisor 2n−1 part of N modulo p^{e−1}).** Because 2p^e = 2p·p^{e−1} ≡ 0 (mod p^{e−1}), (5.1) gives N ≡ d²−1 (mod p^{e−1}). With (C1),

  **p^{e−1} | d²−1 = (d−1)(d+1).**  (5.2)

Since p is odd and the two factors d−1, d+1 differ by 2, p divides at most one of them. As p^{e−1} is a prime power dividing the product (5.2), the **whole** prime power divides the single factor it shares a prime with:

  **p^{e−1} | d−1  or  p^{e−1} | d+1.**  (5.3)

**Step 5b (the (p−1) part).** Reducing (5.1) modulo p−1 and using p≡1 (mod p−1), so p^e≡1:

  N ≡ d² + 2 − 1 = d² + 1 (mod p−1),  so by (C2)  **(p−1) | d²+1.**  (5.4)

---

### Step 6. The composite case forces e=2.

We now turn (5.3) into a bound on b.

**The sub-case d=1.** If d=1 then a=b+1 and

  N = (b+1)²+b²+1 = 2b²+2b+2 = 2(b²+b+1) = 2(ab+1) = 2n.

So N=2n=2p^e. Condition φ(n)|N becomes p^{e−1}(p−1) | 2p^e, i.e. (p−1) | 2p^e / p^{e−1} = 2p. Since also (p−1) | 2(p−1)=2p−2, subtracting gives (p−1) | 2, hence p−1∈{1,2}; as p is an odd prime, **p=3**. Then n=3^e=b²+b+1. But b²+b+1 is never divisible by 9: for b≡0,…,8 (mod 9) the residues of b²+b+1 are 1,3,7,4,3,4,7,3,1, none equal to 0. Hence v₃(b²+b+1)≤1, forcing e=1 — contradicting e≥2. **So d=1 gives no composite solution.**

**The sub-case d≠1.** By (5.3), p^{e−1} divides one of d−1, d+1.

We separate the two possibilities of (5.3) and parametrize. Recall ab=p^e−1=pP−1, where we abbreviate **P:=p^{e−1}** (so P≥p≥3 since e≥2).

*Case I: P | d−1.* Since d=a−b≥0 and d≠1, and d≡1 (mod P) with P≥3, we have d≥P+1>1; write d=1+Pt with integer t≥1. Then a=b+d=b+1+Pt, and ab=pP−1 gives

  (b+1+Pt)·b = pP−1 ⟹ b²+b+Ptb = pP−1 ⟹ **b²+b+1 = P(p−tb).**  (6.1)

The left side is positive, so p−tb≥1; with t≥1 this yields tb≤p−1, hence **b≤p−1.** Moreover P | b²+b+1 by (6.1), so

  P = p^{e−1} | b²+b+1 ≤ (p−1)²+(p−1)+1 = p²−p+1.

If e≥3 then P=p^{e−1}≥p², but p² ≤ P ≤ p²−p+1 < p² is impossible. Hence **e=2** in Case I.

*Case II: P | d+1.* Then d+1≥P (as d+1>0 and P | d+1, d+1≥1 ⟹ d+1≥P), so d=Pj−1 with integer j≥1. Then a=b+Pj−1, and ab=pP−1 gives

  (b+Pj−1)·b = pP−1 ⟹ b²−b+Pjb = pP−1 ⟹ **b²−b+1 = P(p−jb).**  (6.2)

Again positivity gives p−jb≥1, so jb≤p−1 and **b≤p−1.** And P | b²−b+1, so

  P = p^{e−1} | b²−b+1 ≤ (p−1)²−(p−1)+1 = p²−3p+3 < p².

If e≥3 then P≥p²>b²−b+1≥P, impossible. Hence **e=2** in Case II.

In both cases **e=2**, so **n=p².** (Note b=1 lands in Case I: 1²+1+1=3 and p must divide 3, so p=3, consistent with the solution (8,1).)

---

### Step 7. e=2: the only composite solution is (8,1).

Now n=p², P=p, ab=p²−1, and the conditions are (C1) p | N and (C2) (p−1) | N, equivalently (5.4) (p−1) | d²+1. We use the parametrizations (6.1)/(6.2).

**Case I, e=2.** Here (6.1) becomes **b²+b+1 = p(p−tb)**, with t≥1, b≤p−1, and p | b²+b+1. Set

  u := p − tb = (b²+b+1)/p  ≥ 1.

Since b²+b+1 = b(b+1)+1 is **odd**, u is **odd**. Also u=(b²+b+1)/p < (b²+b+1)/b = b+1+1/b, so u≤b+1; and (b+1)∤(b²+b+1) (the remainder is 1), so in fact **u≤b**.

Now translate (5.4). From d=1+pt we have, modulo p−1 (using p≡1), d≡1+t (mod p−1), so by (5.4)

  (p−1) | (1+t)²+1 = (t+1)²+1.

We bound t and p in terms of b and u. From tb=p−u and p=(b²+b+1)/u:

  t = (p−u)/b = (b²+b+1−u²)/(u b),  p−1 = (b²+b+1−u)/u.

Since (t+1)²+1>0, divisibility forces **p−1 ≤ (t+1)²+1.** We show this fails for all u≥2 and, separately, for u=1 with b≥2 — leaving only b=1.

*Sub-case u=1.* Then p=b²+b+1 and t=(b²+b)/b=b+1, so the condition (p−1) | (t+1)²+1 reads

  (b²+b) | (b+2)²+1 = b²+4b+5.

Subtracting the multiple b²+b: (b²+b) | (b²+4b+5)−(b²+b) = 3b+5. For b≥2 we have b²+b > 3b+5 (equivalent to b²−2b−5>0, i.e. b>1+√6≈3.449, and one checks b=2: 6∤11, b=3: 12∤14 directly), so a positive number 3b+5 strictly smaller than b²+b cannot be a multiple of it. Hence **u=1 forces b=1** (indeed b=1: b²+b=2 divides 3b+5=8, giving p=3, a=(p²−1)/b=8, the solution (8,1)).

*Sub-case u≥2.* Since u is odd, u≥3. Recall u≤b, so b≥u≥3. We claim p−1 > (t+1)²+1, which makes (p−1) | (t+1)²+1 impossible. Indeed, multiplying through by the positive quantity u²b² and writing the difference,

  u²b²·[(p−1) − ((t+1)²+1)] = b⁴u − b⁴ − b³u − 2b³ − b²u² − b²u − 3b² + 2bu³ + 2bu² − 2bu − 2b − u⁴ + 2u² − 1 =: Φ(b,u).

(Substitute p−1=(b²+b+1−u)/u and t+1=(b²+b+1−u²+ub)/(ub); the displayed polynomial Φ is the cleared numerator, an exact identity.) We show **Φ(b,u)>0 for all u≥3 and b≥u.** Put b=u+w with w≥0 and expand:

  Φ(u+w,u) = (u−1)w⁴ + (4u²−5u−2)w³ + (6u³−10u²−7u−3)w² + (4u⁴−7u³−6u²−8u−2)w + (u²+1)(u³−2u²−2u−1).

Each coefficient is **positive for u≥3**:
- u−1>0;
- 4u²−5u−2>0 (its larger root is (5+√57)/8≈1.57<3);
- 6u³−10u²−7u−3: at u=3 it is 48>0 and it is increasing for u≥3 (derivative 18u²−20u−7>0 there);
- 4u⁴−7u³−6u²−8u−2: at u=3 it is 55>0 and increasing for u≥3;
- (u²+1)(u³−2u²−2u−1): the cubic factor is positive for u≥3 (u=3 gives 2>0, and it is increasing).

Hence Φ(u+w,u)>0 for all w≥0, i.e. Φ(b,u)>0 for b≥u≥3. Therefore p−1>(t+1)²+1, and (p−1)∤(t+1)²+1. **So u≥2 gives no solution.**

Combining, Case I, e=2 yields **only b=1**, the solution **(8,1)** with a=8, b=1, n=9, φ(9)=6 | 66=N. (Verification: 8²+1²+1=66=11·6.) Both 8=F₆ and 1=F₁ are Fibonacci.

**Case II, e=2.** Here (6.2) becomes **b²−b+1 = p(p−jb)**, with j≥1, b≤p−1, and p | b²−b+1. Set

  u′ := p − jb = (b²−b+1)/p ≥ 1.

Note b²−b+1=b(b−1)+1 is odd, so u′ is odd. Also for b=1, b²−b+1=1, which would force p | 1 — impossible; so **b≥2** in Case II, and u′=(b²−b+1)/p<(b²−b+1)/b=b−1+1/b, hence u′≤b−1.

From d=pj−1 we get, modulo p−1, d≡j−1 (mod p−1), so by (5.4)

  (p−1) | (j−1)²+1.

Again p−1 ≤ (j−1)²+1 is necessary. Writing p=(b²−b+1)/u′, j=(p−u′)/b, the cleared difference is

  (u′)²b²·[(p−1)−((j−1)²+1)] = Ψ(b,u′),

where, with b=u′+w,

  Ψ(u′+w,u′) = (u′−1)w⁴ + (4u′²−3u′+2)w³ + (6u′³−4u′²+5u′−3)w² + (4u′⁴−5u′³+2u′²−4u′+2)w + (u′⁵−4u′⁴−u′³+u′²+2u′−1).

*Sub-case u′=1.* Then p=b²−b+1, j=(p−1)/b=(b²−b)/b=b−1, and the condition reads (b²−b) | (b−2)²+1=b²−4b+5. Subtracting b²−b: (b²−b) | (b²−4b+5)−(b²−b)=−3b+5, i.e. (b²−b) | 3b−5. For b≥2, b²−b≥2 and b²−b>3b−5 (since b²−4b+5>0 always), while 3b−5≥1>0; a positive number smaller than b²−b cannot be a multiple of it. (Check b=2: 2∤1; b=3: 6∤4.) So **u′=1 gives no solution** with b≥2.

*Sub-case u′≥3 (u′ odd, u′≥2 ⟹ u′≥3).* Then b≥u′+1, i.e. w=b−u′≥1. The coefficients of w⁴,w³,w²,w¹ in Ψ(u′+w,u′) are all positive for u′≥3:
- u′−1>0;
- 4u′²−3u′+2>0 (discriminant 9−32<0);
- 6u′³−4u′²+5u′−3>0 (at u′=3 it is 138>0, increasing);
- 4u′⁴−5u′³+2u′²−4u′+2>0 (at u′=3 it is 197>0, increasing).

(The constant term u′⁵−4u′⁴−⋯ may be negative, e.g. at u′=3,4; this is why we use w≥1.) Hence Ψ(u′+w,u′) is **increasing** in w for w≥0, so for w≥1 it is at least its value at w=1, namely the sum of all five coefficients:

  Ψ(u′+1,u′) = u′⁵ + 3u′² + u′ − 1 = (u′+1)²(u′³−2u′²+3u′−1) > 0  for u′≥1.

(The cubic u′³−2u′²+3u′−1 is positive for u′≥1: at u′=1 it is 1, and its derivative 3u′²−4u′+3>0 for all real u′.) Therefore Ψ(b,u′)>0, i.e. p−1>(j−1)²+1, contradicting (p−1) | (j−1)²+1. **So u′≥2 gives no solution.**

Thus Case II, e=2 yields **no composite solution at all.**

---

### Step 8. Conclusion.

Collecting the cases:

- n=2: only (1,1).
- n=p prime (Step 4): all solutions are consecutive odd-index Fibonacci pairs; in our range these are (1,1),(2,1),(5,2),(34,13).
- n=p^e composite, i.e. e≥2 (Steps 5–7): Steps 6–7 forced e=2, and the e=2 analysis produced the **unique** composite solution (8,1) (Case I, u=1, b=1), Case II producing none.

In every solution both a and b are Fibonacci numbers:

  1=F₁, 2=F₃, 5=F₅, 8=F₆, 13=F₇, 34=F₉,

and these exhaust the solution list (1,1),(2,1),(5,2),(8,1),(34,13) (up to the symmetry a↔b). Hence whenever φ(ab+1) | a²+b²+1 with a,b positive integers, both a and b are Fibonacci numbers. ∎
