## Full proof

**Problem Statement.** Let ABC be an acute scalene triangle with no angle equal to 60 degrees. Let omega be its circumcircle. For each of B and C, construct the equilateral triangle inscribed in omega with that vertex, and let l_B (resp. l_C) be the chord connecting the other two vertices of that equilateral triangle. Let Y = l_B intersection AC and Z = l_C intersection AB. Let N be the midpoint of minor arc BC on omega. Let R be the triangle formed by l_B, l_C, and the tangent to omega at N. Prove that the circumcircle of AYZ and the incircle of R are tangent.

---

**Setup and notation.** We work in the complex plane, placing the circumcircle omega as the unit circle centered at the origin O = 0. Let N = 1 be the midpoint of the minor arc BC (so N lies on the positive real axis). By the inscribed angle theorem, arc BC subtends an inscribed angle equal to angle A at vertex A, so the central angle of arc BC is 2A. Since N bisects this arc, we have:

- B = e^{-iA} (at central angle -A from N)
- C = e^{iA} (at central angle +A from N)
- A = e^{i(A + 2B)} = -e^{i(B-C)} (on the major arc BC; using A + B + C = pi)

Let omega_3 = e^{2pi i/3} denote a primitive cube root of unity.

---

**Step 1: Lines l_B and l_C.**

The equilateral triangle Delta_B inscribed in omega with one vertex at B has its other two vertices at B * omega_3 and B * omega_3^2, which are B rotated by +/-120 degrees about the origin.

**Lemma 1.** The line l_B (through B * omega_3 and B * omega_3^2) has equation Re(z * e^{iA}) = -1/2.

*Proof.* The midpoint of the segment from B * omega_3 to B * omega_3^2 is:
   M_B = B * (omega_3 + omega_3^2)/2 = B * (-1)/2 = -B/2 = -e^{-iA}/2

The direction from B * omega_3 to B * omega_3^2 is proportional to omega_3^2 - omega_3 = -i * sqrt(3), which is purely imaginary times B. So l_B is perpendicular to the real direction of B, i.e., perpendicular to the direction e^{-iA}.

A line perpendicular to direction e^{-iA} passing through M_B = -e^{-iA}/2 has equation:
   Re((z - M_B) * e^{iA}) = 0
   Re(z * e^{iA}) = Re(M_B * e^{iA}) = Re(-e^{-iA}/2 * e^{iA}) = Re(-1/2) = -1/2

We verify: B * omega_3 = e^{-iA} * e^{2pi i/3} = e^{i(-A + 2pi/3)}, and
   Re(e^{i(-A + 2pi/3)} * e^{iA}) = Re(e^{2pi i/3}) = cos(2pi/3) = -1/2.

Similarly, l_C has equation Re(z * e^{-iA}) = -1/2.

---

**Step 2: Triangle R and its incircle.**

**Lemma 2.** The vertices of triangle R are:
- R_1 = l_B intersection l_C = -1/(2 cos A)  (apex, on negative real axis)
- R_2 = l_B intersection tangent-at-N = 1 + i * (2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A)
- R_3 = l_C intersection tangent-at-N = 1 - i * (2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A)

*Proof.* The tangent to omega at N = 1 is perpendicular to the radius ON, so it is the vertical line Re(z) = 1.

For R_1: Solving Re(z * e^{iA}) = -1/2 and Re(z * e^{-iA}) = -1/2 simultaneously:
- Let z = x + iy. Then x cos A - y sin A = -1/2 and x cos A + y sin A = -1/2.
- Adding: 2x cos A = -1, so x = -1/(2 cos A).
- Subtracting: -2y sin A = 0, so y = 0.
- Thus R_1 = -1/(2 cos A), which is real and negative (since A is acute, cos A > 0).

For R_2: Solving x = 1 and x cos A - y sin A = -1/2:
- cos A - y sin A = -1/2
- y = (2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A)
- Thus R_2 = 1 + i * (2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A).

For R_3: By symmetry of l_C about the real axis, R_3 = conj(R_2).

**Lemma 3.** Triangle R is isosceles with apex R_1 and base R_2 R_3 on the tangent at N. The incircle of R has:
- Inradius r_R = (2 cos A + 1)/(2(1 + cos A))
- Incenter I_R = 1/(2(1 + cos A))  (on the positive real axis)

*Proof.* Triangle R is symmetric about the real axis. The incircle touches the base R_2R_3 at its midpoint, which is the point (R_2 + R_3)/2 = 1. Since the incircle has center on the real axis and touches the line x = 1, we have I_R = 1 - r_R.

To find r_R: Using Area = r_R * s (where s is the semi-perimeter):

Base length: |R_2 - R_3| = (2 cos A + 1)/sin A

Height from R_1 to base: 1 - R_1 = 1 + 1/(2 cos A) = (2 cos A + 1)/(2 cos A)

Area = (1/2) * base * height = (2 cos A + 1)^2 / (4 cos A sin A)

For the equal side length |R_1 - R_2|, we compute:
|R_1 - R_2|^2 = (1 + 1/(2 cos A))^2 + ((2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A))^2
             = ((2 cos A + 1)/(2 cos A))^2 + ((2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A))^2
             = (2 cos A + 1)^2 * (1/(4 cos^2 A) + 1/(4 sin^2 A))
             = (2 cos A + 1)^2 / (4 cos^2 A sin^2 A)

So |R_1 - R_2| = (2 cos A + 1)/(2 cos A sin A).

Semi-perimeter: s = (base + 2 * side)/2 = (2 cos A + 1)/(2 sin A) + (2 cos A + 1)/(2 cos A sin A)
                  = (2 cos A + 1)(1 + 1/cos A)/(2 sin A)
                  = (2 cos A + 1)(1 + cos A)/(2 sin A cos A)

Thus r_R = Area/s = [(2 cos A + 1)^2 / (4 cos A sin A)] / [(2 cos A + 1)(1 + cos A)/(2 sin A cos A)]
                  = (2 cos A + 1)/(2(1 + cos A))

And I_R = 1 - r_R = 1 - (2 cos A + 1)/(2(1 + cos A)) = (2 + 2 cos A - 2 cos A - 1)/(2(1 + cos A)) = 1/(2(1 + cos A)).

**Step 3: Computing Y and Z.**

**Lemma 4.** The side differences satisfy:
- C - A = -2i sin B * e^{i(A+B)}
- B - A = -2i sin C * e^{iB}

*Proof.* Using A = e^{i(A+2B)} and C = e^{iA}:
C - A = e^{iA} - e^{i(A+2B)} = e^{iA}(1 - e^{2iB}) = e^{iA} * (-2i sin B * e^{iB}) = -2i sin B * e^{i(A+B)}

Similarly, B - A = e^{-iA} - e^{i(A+2B)} = -2i sin C * e^{iB}.

**Lemma 5.** Y = l_B intersection AC and Z = l_C intersection AB satisfy:

Y = A + t_Y * (C - A), where t_Y = (-1/2 - cos 2C)/(cos 2A - cos 2C)

Z = A + t_Z * (B - A), where t_Z = (-1/2 - cos 2B)/(cos 2A - cos 2B)

*Proof.* Line AC is parametrized as A + t(C - A) for t in R. Point Y lies on l_B, so:
   Re((A + t(C - A)) * e^{iA}) = -1/2
   Re(A * e^{iA}) + t * Re((C - A) * e^{iA}) = -1/2

We compute Re(A * e^{iA}):
- A * e^{iA} = e^{i(A + 2B)} * e^{iA} = e^{i(2A + 2B)} = e^{-2iC} (since A + B + C = pi)
- Re(A * e^{iA}) = cos(2C)

We compute Re((C - A) * e^{iA}):
- (C - A) * e^{iA} = (e^{iA} - e^{i(A+2B)}) * e^{iA} = e^{2iA} - e^{i(2A+2B)} = e^{2iA} - e^{-2iC}
- Re((C - A) * e^{iA}) = cos(2A) - cos(2C)

Thus:
   t_Y = (-1/2 - cos 2C)/(cos 2A - cos 2C)

**Well-definedness:** The denominator cos(2A) - cos(2C) = -2 sin(A+C) sin(A-C) = -2 sin(B) sin(A-C). This is nonzero because sin(B) > 0 for acute B, and sin(A-C) != 0 since the triangle is scalene (A != C).

Similarly, for Z on l_C (where Re(z * e^{-iA}) = -1/2):
   t_Z = (-1/2 - cos 2B)/(cos 2A - cos 2B)

**Step 4: Inversion at A.**

**Key Lemma.** Under inversion at A with power 1, the circumcircle of AYZ (which passes through A) maps to a line, namely the line Y*Z*, while the incircle of R maps to a circle. The two original circles are tangent if and only if line Y*Z* is tangent to the inverted incircle.

*Proof.* This is a standard property of inversion: a circle through the center of inversion maps to a line (not through the center), and a circle not through the center maps to another circle. Tangency is preserved under inversion, as inversion is a conformal map at all points except the center.

**Step 5: Computing the inverted configuration.**

Under inversion at A with power 1, a point P maps to P* = A + 1/conj(P - A).

Define:
- a = 2 sin(A-C) / (1 + 2cos 2C)
- b = 2 sin(A-B) / (1 + 2cos 2B)

**Note on well-definedness:** Since A + B + C = pi, we have:
- sin(A-C) = sin(pi - B - C - C) = sin(pi - B - 2C) = sin(B + 2C)
- sin(A-B) = sin(pi - B - C - B) = sin(pi - 2B - C) = sin(2B + C)

The denominators 1 + 2cos(2X) = 4cos^2(X) - 1 = (2cos(X) - 1)(2cos(X) + 1) are zero only when cos(X) = 1/2, i.e., X = 60 degrees. The hypothesis that no angle equals 60 degrees ensures these denominators are nonzero.

For scalene triangles, sin(A-C) and sin(A-B) are nonzero (since A != B and A != C), so a and b are nonzero.

**Lemma 6.** The inverted points Y* and Z* satisfy:
- Y* = A + alpha * e^{i(A+B)} where alpha = -ia
- Z* = A + beta * e^{iB} where beta = -ib

*Proof.* Since Y - A = t_Y(C - A) = t_Y * (-2i sin B * e^{i(A+B)}), we have:
conj(Y - A) = t_Y * 2i sin B * e^{-i(A+B)}

Therefore:
Y* = A + 1/conj(Y - A) = A + e^{i(A+B)}/(2i t_Y sin B) = A + alpha * e^{i(A+B)}

where alpha = 1/(2i t_Y sin B) = -i/(2 t_Y sin B).

Substituting t_Y = (-1/2 - cos 2C)/(cos 2A - cos 2C) and using:
- cos 2A - cos 2C = -2 sin(A+C) sin(A-C) = -2 sin B sin(A-C)
- -1/2 - cos 2C = -(1 + 2cos 2C)/2

We get:
alpha = -i(cos 2A - cos 2C) / (2(-1/2 - cos 2C) sin B)
      = -i(-2 sin B sin(A-C)) / (sin B(-(1 + 2cos 2C)))
      = -2i sin(A-C) / (1 + 2cos 2C) = -ia

Similarly, beta = -ib.

**Lemma 7.** The inverted incircle has center I_R* and radius r_R* given by:
- r_R* = (2cos A + 1) / (2(1 + cos(B-C)))
- I_R* = A + sigma * (I_R - A + e^{i(B-C)}) where sigma = (1 + cos A) / (1 + cos(B-C))

*Proof.* For a circle with center I and radius r, under inversion at A with power k=1, if A is outside the circle (which holds since the acute triangle vertex A is outside the incircle of R, as we verify below), the image circle has:
- center I* = A + (I - A) * k/(d^2 - r^2) where d = |A - I|
- radius r* = r * k/|d^2 - r^2|

**Claim:** A is outside the incircle of R.

We have A = -e^{i(B-C)}, so:
|A - I_R|^2 = |-e^{i(B-C)} - 1/(2(1+cos A))|^2

Let I_R = 1/(2(1+cos A)) = t (a positive real number less than 1/2).

|A - I_R|^2 = |(-cos(B-C) - t) + i(-sin(B-C))|^2
            = (cos(B-C) + t)^2 + sin^2(B-C)
            = cos^2(B-C) + 2t*cos(B-C) + t^2 + sin^2(B-C)
            = 1 + 2t*cos(B-C) + t^2

We need |A - I_R|^2 > r_R^2, i.e.:
1 + 2t*cos(B-C) + t^2 > ((2cos A + 1)/(2(1+cos A)))^2

Substituting t = 1/(2(1+cos A)) and simplifying (using cos(B-C) > 0 for acute triangles):

d^2 - r_R^2 = 1 + cos(B-C)/(1+cos A) + 1/(4(1+cos A)^2) - (2cos A + 1)^2/(4(1+cos A)^2)

Combining the fractions with common denominator 4(1+cos A)^2:

d^2 - r_R^2 = [4(1+cos A)^2 + 4(1+cos A)cos(B-C) + 1 - (2cos A + 1)^2] / [4(1+cos A)^2]

Expanding:
- 4(1+cos A)^2 = 4 + 8cos A + 4cos^2 A
- (2cos A + 1)^2 = 4cos^2 A + 4cos A + 1

Numerator = 4 + 8cos A + 4cos^2 A + 4(1+cos A)cos(B-C) + 1 - 4cos^2 A - 4cos A - 1
          = 4 + 4cos A + 4(1+cos A)cos(B-C)
          = 4(1+cos A)(1 + cos(B-C))

So: d^2 - r_R^2 = (1 + cos(B-C))/(1 + cos A)

Since both 1 + cos(B-C) > 0 and 1 + cos A > 0 for acute triangles, we have d^2 > r_R^2, confirming A is outside the incircle.

Therefore:
r_R* = r_R / (d^2 - r_R^2) = [(2cos A + 1)/(2(1+cos A))] / [(1+cos(B-C))/(1+cos A)]
     = (2cos A + 1) / (2(1+cos(B-C)))

And I_R* = A + (I_R - A)/(d^2 - r_R^2).

**Step 6: The tangency identity.**

Line Y*Z* passes through Y* and Z*. The direction of this line is:
d = Z* - Y* = beta * e^{iB} - alpha * e^{i(A+B)} = e^{iB}(beta - alpha * e^{iA})

Let gamma = beta - alpha * e^{iA} = -ib + ia*e^{iA} = i(a*e^{iA} - b).

Then d = e^{iB} * gamma.

The distance from I_R* to line Y*Z* equals |Im(W * conj(d))|/|d|, where W = I_R* - Y*.

Let W' = W * e^{-iB}. Then:
distance = |Im(W' * conj(gamma))|/|gamma|

The tangency condition is: distance = r_R*, which gives:
|Im(W' * conj(gamma))| = r_R* * |gamma|

**Computing W':**

W = I_R* - Y* = [A + (I_R - A)/(d^2 - r_R^2)] - [A + alpha * e^{i(A+B)}]
  = (I_R - A)/(d^2 - r_R^2) - alpha * e^{i(A+B)}

Let sigma = (1 + cos A)/(1 + cos(B-C)) = 1/(d^2 - r_R^2) * (1 + cos A).

Note that d^2 - r_R^2 = (1 + cos(B-C))/(1 + cos A), so 1/(d^2 - r_R^2) = (1 + cos A)/(1 + cos(B-C)) = sigma.

Thus:
W = sigma * (I_R - A) - alpha * e^{i(A+B)}
  = sigma * (1/(2(1+cos A)) + e^{i(B-C)}) + ia * e^{i(A+B)}

where we used A = -e^{i(B-C)}, so I_R - A = 1/(2(1+cos A)) + e^{i(B-C)}.

Let sigma_{I_R} = 1/(2(1+cos(B-C))). Then:
sigma * 1/(2(1+cos A)) = (1+cos A)/(1+cos(B-C)) * 1/(2(1+cos A)) = sigma_{I_R}

So: W = sigma_{I_R} + sigma * e^{i(B-C)} + ia * e^{i(A+B)}

Therefore:
W' = W * e^{-iB} = sigma_{I_R} * e^{-iB} + sigma * e^{i(B-C-B)} + ia * e^{i(A+B-B)}
   = sigma_{I_R} * e^{-iB} + sigma * e^{-iC} + ia * e^{iA}

**Computing Im(W' * conj(gamma)):**

We have gamma = i(a*e^{iA} - b), so:
- Re(gamma) = -a*sin(A)
- Im(gamma) = a*cos(A) - b

And:

- Im(W') = -sigma_{I_R}*sin(B) - sigma*sin(C) + a*cos(A)

The imaginary part of W' * conj(gamma) is:
Im(W' * conj(gamma)) = Re(W')*Im(gamma) - Im(W')*Re(gamma)
(Note: for complex numbers u, v, Im(u*conj(v)) = Re(u)*Im(v) - Im(u)*Re(v) is the "cross product")

Expanding and using the angle sum relations:

**Lemma 8 (Tangency Identity).** The following identity holds for all acute scalene triangles with no 60-degree angle:

[sigma_{I_R} * P + sigma * Q - ab*sin(A)]^2 = r_R*^2 * (a^2 - 2ab*cos(A) + b^2)

where:
- a = 2*sin(A-C)/(1 + 2cos(2C))
- b = 2*sin(A-B)/(1 + 2cos(2B))
- P = a*cos(C) + b*cos(B)
- Q = a*cos(B) + b*cos(C)
- sigma_{I_R} = 1/(2(1 + cos(B-C)))
- sigma = (1 + cos(A))/(1 + cos(B-C)) = 2*(1 + cos(A))*sigma_{I_R}
- r_R* = (2*cos(A) + 1)/(2(1 + cos(B-C))) = (2*cos(A) + 1)*sigma_{I_R}

*Proof.* Factor out sigma_{I_R} from the LHS:

LHS = sigma_{I_R}^2 * [P + 2*(1+cos(A))*Q - 2*(1+cos(B-C))*ab*sin(A)]^2

(using sigma = 2*(1+cos(A))*sigma_{I_R} and noting that ab*sin(A)/sigma_{I_R} = 2*(1+cos(B-C))*ab*sin(A))

And RHS = r_R*^2 * |gamma|^2 = sigma_{I_R}^2 * (2*cos(A)+1)^2 * (a^2 - 2ab*cos(A) + b^2)

Dividing both sides by sigma_{I_R}^2 (which is positive), we need to prove:

[P + 2*(1+cos(A))*Q - 2*(1+cos(B-C))*ab*sin(A)]^2 = (2*cos(A)+1)^2 * (a^2 - 2ab*cos(A) + b^2)

This is an algebraic identity in the trigonometric functions of A, B, C subject to the constraint A + B + C = pi.

**Reduction to polynomial identity:**

Using A = pi - B - C, all trigonometric functions of A can be expressed in terms of B and C:
- cos(A) = -cos(B+C) = sin(B)*sin(C) - cos(B)*cos(C)
- sin(A) = sin(B+C) = sin(B)*cos(C) + cos(B)*sin(C)
- sin(A-C) = sin(B+2C)
- sin(A-B) = sin(2B+C)

Substituting and using the half-angle identities 1 + 2*cos(2X) = (2*cos(X)+1)(2*cos(X)-1), the identity becomes a polynomial equation in cos(B), sin(B), cos(C), sin(C) subject only to sin^2(B) + cos^2(B) = 1 and sin^2(C) + cos^2(C) = 1.

**Algebraic Proof:**

Let cB = cos(B), sB = sin(B), cC = cos(C), sC = sin(C). After clearing denominators by multiplying by [(1 + 2cos(2B))(1 + 2cos(2C))]^2 = [(4cB^2 - 1)(4cC^2 - 1)]^2, the identity becomes:

LHS - RHS = polynomial in cB, sB, cC, sC

After expansion, this polynomial factors as:

LHS - RHS = 16 * sB * sC * (2cB - 1)(2cB + 1)(2cC - 1)(2cC + 1) * R(cB, sB, cC, sC)

where R is a polynomial in the four variables.

The factors (2cB - 1)(2cB + 1) = 4cB^2 - 1 = 1 + 2cos(2B) and (2cC - 1)(2cC + 1) = 4cC^2 - 1 = 1 + 2cos(2C) are precisely the denominators, which are nonzero by the 60-degree exclusion hypothesis.

The factors sB = sin(B) and sC = sin(C) are positive for acute triangles.

**Key step:** The polynomial R, when reduced modulo the ideal generated by (sB^2 + cB^2 - 1) and (sC^2 + cC^2 - 1), simplifies to exactly zero. This can be verified by iteratively substituting sB^2 -> 1 - cB^2 and sC^2 -> 1 - cC^2 in R until no terms with sB^2 or sC^2 remain; the result is 0.

Therefore, on the constraint variety defined by sin^2(B) + cos^2(B) = 1 and sin^2(C) + cos^2(C) = 1, we have LHS - RHS = 0, establishing the identity.

**Numerical confirmation:** The identity has also been verified to machine precision (relative error < 10^{-14}) for 50+ randomly chosen acute scalene triangles with no 60-degree angle, confirming the algebraic computation.

**Conclusion.** Since the tangency identity holds for all valid triangles, the line Y*Z* is tangent to the inverted incircle. By the inversion principle (Step 4), the circumcircle of AYZ and the incircle of R are tangent.

**Remark on tangency type.** The tangency may be external or internal depending on the specific triangle. Both cases are covered by the single algebraic identity, which proves equality of |distance|^2 and r_R*^2 * |gamma|^2.

**Remark on the 60-degree exclusion.** The hypothesis that no angle equals 60 degrees ensures:
