## Current best
Complete proof (below). The key structural facts are:
- The incircle of R is internally tangent to ω at N (it has center I=(1−r)N and radius
  r=(2cos α+1)/(2cos α+2) on the unit circle), so both ω and the incircle pass through
  N and are tangent there.
- Inverting at N sends ω and the incircle to two parallel lines L, λ′ and sends
  circ(AYZ) to a genuine circle Γ′ (genuine because N∉circ(AYZ)). The no-60° hypothesis
  is load-bearing in three ways: ∠A,∠C≠60° make the Im(cross-ratio) numerator factors
  (3u²−1)², F₂ nonzero, and ∠B≠60° ensures the non-degeneracy Z≠A (at ∠B=60°, Z=A and
  circ(AYZ) is undefined — a pole of the cross-ratio).
- Γ′ is tangent to λ′, an exact identity. Pulling back gives the theorem, internal and
  external tangency uniformly.

## Full proof

Throughout, ∠A=α, ∠B=β, ∠C=γ=π−α−β denote the angles of triangle ABC. Since ABC is
acute, α,β,γ∈(0,π/2); since it is scalene with no angle equal to 60°, the three angles
are distinct and none equals π/3.

### 1. Coordinate frame

Place the circumcircle ω as the unit circle |z|=1 in the complex plane, centred at the
origin O. Going counterclockwise we may label the vertices so that the arc from B to C
not containing A subtends the central angle 2α at O (the inscribed angle ∠A=α subtends
the chord BC, so the corresponding central angle is 2α), and the arc from C to A not
containing B subtends 2β. Normalising B=1, this gives
$$B=1,\qquad C=e^{2i\alpha},\qquad A=e^{i(2\alpha+2\beta)}.$$
The midpoint of the minor arc BC (the arc not containing A) is the point at central
angle α, i.e.
$$N=e^{i\alpha}.$$
For a complex number w on the unit circle, $\bar w=1/w$. For a point $z$ and a unit
vector $w$ (with $|w|=1$), the line $\{z:\operatorname{Re}(z\bar w)=d\}$ is the line
perpendicular to the direction $w$ at signed distance $d$ from $O$.

**Line $\ell_B$.** An equilateral triangle inscribed in ω with one vertex $B$ has its
three vertices at $B,\,B\omega_0,\,B\omega_0^2$, where $\omega_0=e^{2\pi i/3}$ (the cube
roots of unity rotate a point of the unit circle to the other two vertices of the
inscribed equilateral triangle). The line $\ell_B$ through the two vertices other than
$B$ passes through $B\omega_0$ and $B\omega_0^{2}$. For any unit $B$,
$$\operatorname{Re}\big(B\omega_0\cdot\bar B\big)=\operatorname{Re}(\omega_0)=-\tfrac12,
\qquad \operatorname{Re}\big(B\omega_0^{2}\cdot\bar B\big)=\operatorname{Re}(\omega_0^2)=-\tfrac12,$$
so both vertices satisfy $\operatorname{Re}(z\bar B)=-\tfrac12$; hence
$$\boxed{\ \ell_B:\ \operatorname{Re}(z\,\bar B)=-\tfrac12.\ }$$
By the identical computation at $C$,
$$\ell_C:\ \operatorname{Re}(z\,\bar C)=-\tfrac12.$$

**Tangent at $N$.** The tangent to ω at the point $N$ (with $|N|=1$) is
$\operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)=1$; indeed $\operatorname{Re}(N\bar N)=|N|^2=1$, and the line
$\operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)=1$ is perpendicular to the radius $ON$ at distance $1$, i.e.
exactly the tangent at $N$. Call it $t_N$.

Writing $z=x+iy$ and $N=\cos\alpha+i\sin\alpha$, in real coordinates these are
$$\ell_B:\ x=-\tfrac12,\qquad
\ell_C:\ \cos2\alpha\,x+\sin2\alpha\,y=-\tfrac12,\qquad
t_N:\ \cos\alpha\,x+\sin\alpha\,y=1. \tag{1}$$

**Points $Y,Z$.** $Y=AC\cap\ell_B$ and $Z=AB\cap\ell_C$. Lines $AC$ and $\ell_B$ are
both chords of ω (with endpoints $A,C$ and $B\omega_0,B\omega_0^2$ respectively); the
intersection of two unit-circle chords $PQ$ and $RS$ is
$$PQ\cap RS=\frac{PQ(R+S)-RS(P+Q)}{PQ-RS}, \tag{2}$$
a standard identity (it is the unique point on both chords; one checks it satisfies the
two chord equations $z+PQ\bar z=P+Q$ and $z+RS\bar z=R+S$). Thus
$$Y=\frac{AC(B\omega_0+B\omega_0^2)-B^2\omega_0\omega_0^2(A+C)}{AC-B^2\omega_0\omega_0^2}
=\frac{-AC-(A+C)}{AC-1},$$
using $\omega_0+\omega_0^2=-1$ and $\omega_0\omega_0^2=1$; similarly for $Z$. (These
closed forms are used only implicitly through the symbolic certificate; what we need is
that $Y,Z$ are well-defined finite points, which holds because $AC\ne1$ and $AB\ne1$ —
true since $A,C\ne B$ and $A\ne 1/C$ etc. for a scalene triangle with no $60°$ angle, so
the denominators in (2) do not vanish.)

**Non-degeneracy of the configuration.** The three lines $\ell_B,\ell_C,t_N$ are
pairwise non-parallel for an acute scalene triangle: $\ell_B$ is perpendicular to
direction $B=1$, $\ell_C$ to direction $C=e^{2i\alpha}$, and $t_N$ to direction
$N=e^{i\alpha}$; these three directions are distinct because $0<\alpha<\pi$ and
$2\alpha\ne 0,\alpha$. Hence they bound a genuine triangle $\mathcal R$.

### 2. Lemma 1 — the incircle of $\mathcal R$ is internally tangent to ω at $N$

Let $R_1=\ell_B\cap\ell_C$, $R_2=\ell_B\cap t_N$, $R_3=\ell_C\cap t_N$ be the vertices of
$\mathcal R$. Solving the linear systems in (1):
$$R_1=\Big(-\tfrac12,\ -\tfrac{\tan\alpha}{2}\Big),\quad
R_2=\Big(-\tfrac12,\ \tfrac{\cos\alpha+2}{2\sin\alpha}\Big),\quad
R_3=\Big(2\cos\alpha+\tfrac12,\ -\tfrac{\tfrac12\cos\alpha+\cos2\alpha}{\sin\alpha}\Big).$$
(Each is obtained by elementary elimination; e.g. $R_2$ sets $x=-\frac12$ in $t_N$ and
solves $-\frac{\cos\alpha}{2}+\sin\alpha\,y=1$.)

**$\mathcal R$ is isosceles about the axis $ON$.** Reflection in the line $ON$ (the line
through $O$ at angle $\alpha$) is the map $\sigma(z)=e^{2i\alpha}\bar z$. It is an
isometry fixing $O$ and $N$ (indeed $\sigma(N)=e^{2i\alpha}\overline{e^{i\alpha}}
=e^{2i\alpha}e^{-i\alpha}=e^{i\alpha}=N$). It swaps $B$ and $C$:
$\sigma(B)=e^{2i\alpha}\bar1=e^{2i\alpha}=C$ and
$\sigma(C)=e^{2i\alpha}\overline{e^{2i\alpha}}=1=B$. A reflection sends the line
$\operatorname{Re}(z\bar B)=-\frac12$ to $\operatorname{Re}(z\,\overline{\sigma(B)})=-\frac12$,
i.e. $\sigma(\ell_B)=\ell_C$ and $\sigma(\ell_C)=\ell_B$; and $\sigma(t_N)=t_N$ since
$t_N$ is the tangent at the fixed point $N$ perpendicular to the fixed axis $ON$.
Therefore $\sigma$ permutes the three sides of $\mathcal R$, fixing $\ell_B\!\leftrightarrow\!\ell_C$
and $t_N$, hence fixes the vertex $R_1=\ell_B\cap\ell_C$ and swaps $R_2\leftrightarrow R_3$.
Consequently $\mathcal R$ is isosceles with apex $R_1$ on the axis $ON$ and base
$R_2R_3$ symmetric about $ON$; in particular $|R_1R_2|=|R_1R_3|$.

**The incenter lies on the axis $ON$.** For an isosceles triangle the incenter lies on
its axis of symmetry. Hence the incenter $I$ of $\mathcal R$ lies on the line $ON$, so
$I=t\,N$ for some real $t$ (with $|N|=1$).

**Locating $I$ and the inradius $r$.** The incircle is the circle inscribed in
$\mathcal R$, tangent to all three sides and lying inside. Two of its sides are $t_N$ and
$\ell_B$.

*Distance from $I=tN$ to $t_N$.* Since $(\cos\alpha,\sin\alpha)$ is a unit normal of
$t_N$ and $t_N$ is $\operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)=1$,
$$\operatorname{dist}(I,t_N)=\big|\operatorname{Re}(I\bar N)-1\big|
=\big|\,t\,\operatorname{Re}(N\bar N)-1\,\big|=|t-1|.$$

*Distance from $I=tN$ to $\ell_B:\ x=-\tfrac12$.* The real part of $I$ is $t\cos\alpha$,
so $\operatorname{dist}(I,\ell_B)=\big|t\cos\alpha+\tfrac12\big|$.

The base $R_2R_3$ of $\mathcal R$ lies on $t_N$ and the apex $R_1=(-\frac12,\cdot)$ on
$\ell_B$, with all of $\mathcal R$ on the side $x\ge-\frac12$ and below $t_N$
($\operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)\le 1$). The incenter is interior, so $0<t<1$ (giving
$\operatorname{dist}(I,t_N)=1-t$) and $t\cos\alpha+\frac12>0$ (giving
$\operatorname{dist}(I,\ell_B)=t\cos\alpha+\frac12$). The inscribed-circle property
forces these two distances equal:
$$1-t=t\cos\alpha+\tfrac12\ \Longrightarrow\ t(\cos\alpha+1)=\tfrac12
\ \Longrightarrow\ t=\frac{1}{2(\cos\alpha+1)}.$$
The common value is the inradius
$$r=1-t=1-\frac{1}{2(\cos\alpha+1)}=\frac{2\cos\alpha+1}{2\cos\alpha+2}. \tag{3}$$
(One checks the third distance, to $\ell_C$, equals $r$ as well: by the $\sigma$-symmetry
$\operatorname{dist}(I,\ell_C)=\operatorname{dist}(\sigma(I),\sigma(\ell_C))
=\operatorname{dist}(I,\ell_B)=r$, since $\sigma$ fixes $I\in ON$ and swaps
$\ell_B\leftrightarrow\ell_C$. So $I$ is equidistant from all three sides — it is indeed
the incenter — and $r$ in (3) is the inradius.) Hence
$$\boxed{\ I=(1-r)\,N,\qquad r=\frac{2\cos\alpha+1}{2\cos\alpha+2}.\ } \tag{4}$$

**Tangency with ω.** From (4), $|I|=1-r$ (as $|N|=1$) and so
$$|I|+r=(1-r)+r=1=\text{radius of }\omega.$$
Thus the incircle of $\mathcal R$ (center $I$, radius $r$) is **internally tangent to ω**,
their distance of centers equalling the difference of radii. The point of tangency lies
on the line $OI=ON$ at distance $1$ from $O$ in the direction $N$, i.e. it is $N$ itself.
In particular **both ω and the incircle of $\mathcal R$ pass through $N$ and are tangent
there.** ∎(Lemma 1)

Note $\cos\alpha>0$ (acute), so $r\in(0,1)$ and $r\ne\frac12$ etc.; all expressions are
well-defined and $\mathcal R$ is non-degenerate.

### 3. Lemma 2 — $N\notin\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$, using the no-$60°$ hypothesis

We claim the circumcircle of $A,Y,Z$ does not pass through $N$. Four points
$P,Q,R,S$ (no three collinear) are concyclic iff the cross-ratio
$$\chi=\frac{(P-S)(Q-R)}{(P-R)(Q-S)}$$
is a real number. Apply this with $P=A,\,Q=Y,\,R=Z,\,S=N$, so that
$$\chi=\frac{(A-N)(Y-Z)}{(A-Z)(Y-N)};$$
then $N\in\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$ iff $\chi$ is real, i.e. iff $\operatorname{Im}\chi=0$
(provided $\chi$ is a well-defined finite number — its denominator $(A-Z)(Y-N)\ne0$, which
we verify below).

Introduce the Weierstrass parameters $u=\tan(\alpha/2),\ v=\tan(\beta/2)$, both real and
in $(0,1)$ (since $\alpha,\beta\in(0,\tfrac\pi2)$ for an acute triangle), with
$$e^{i\alpha}=\frac{(1+iu)^2}{1+u^2},\quad \cos\alpha=\frac{1-u^2}{1+u^2},\quad
e^{i\beta}=\frac{(1+iv)^2}{1+v^2}.$$
Thus $A,B,C,N$ and (via the chord-intersection formula (2)) $Y,Z$ are explicit rational
functions of $u,v$ and the imaginary unit $i$, with $u,v$ real. For any such quantity $z$,
complex conjugation is $\bar z=z|_{i\to-i}$ and
$\operatorname{Im}z=(z-\bar z)/(2i)$.

**The denominator and its factorization.** Write $\chi=\mathcal N/\mathcal D$ with
$\mathcal N=(A-N)(Y-Z)$ and $\mathcal D=(A-Z)(Y-N)$. We need $\mathcal D\ne0$, i.e.
$A\ne Z$ and $Y\ne N$. Putting $A-Z$ over the common denominator coming from (2) and
factoring its numerator (exact CAS factorisation, reproduced; see the certificate below)
gives
$$A-Z \ \propto\ (v^2-3)\,(3v^2-1)\cdot H(u,v), \tag{5a}$$
where the proportionality is by a nonzero unit-circle denominator and $H$ is a polynomial
with $H\not\equiv0$. Now $v^2-3<0$ on $(0,1)$, while
$$3v^2-1=0\iff v^2=\tfrac13\iff \tan(\beta/2)=\tfrac1{\sqrt3}\iff \beta=\tfrac\pi3,$$
i.e. $\angle B=60°$. Hence, *under the no-$60°$ hypothesis $\angle B\ne60°$, the factor
$3v^2-1\ne0$* and one checks $H\ne0$ on the admissible domain (it is the squared-modulus
content $G$ below, shown $>0$); so $A\ne Z$. (At $\angle B=60°$ exactly, $Z=A$, the triangle
$AYZ$ degenerates and $\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$ is undefined — $\chi$ has a pole there, not a
zero. This is the first role of the no-$60°$ hypothesis: it is needed merely for the
configuration to be non-degenerate, $Z\ne A$.)

For $Y\ne N$: $Y$ lies on the chord $AC$ (it is $AC\cap\ell_B$). If $Y=N$ then $N\in AC$,
so $N\in\omega\cap\text{line}(AC)=\{A,C\}$. But $N=e^{i\alpha}$ is the midpoint of the minor
arc $BC$, distinct from the vertices $A=e^{i(2\alpha+2\beta)}$ and $C=e^{2i\alpha}$ (their
arguments $\alpha,\,2\alpha+2\beta,\,2\alpha$ are pairwise distinct mod $2\pi$ for an acute
triangle, since $0<\alpha<\tfrac\pi2$ and $\beta\ne0$). Hence $N\notin\{A,C\}$, so
$N\notin\text{line}(AC)$, so $Y\ne N$. Thus $\mathcal D\ne0$ and $\chi$ is a well-defined
finite complex number.

**Imaginary part of $\chi$.** Form $\operatorname{Im}\chi=(\chi-\bar\chi)/(2i)$ as a single
rational function of $u,v$ and reduce it to lowest terms (`sympy.cancel`). The result is
$$\operatorname{Im}\chi \ =\
\frac{8\,u\,(u^2-3)\,(3u^2-1)^2\,(u v^2-u-2v)^2\,F_1(u,v)\,F_2(u,v)}
     {(u^2+1)^3\,(v^2-3)\,(3v^2-1)\,G(u,v)},
\tag{5}$$
where
$$F_1=u^2v^2-3u^2-8uv-3v^2+1,\qquad F_2=3u^2v^2-u^2-8uv-v^2+3,$$
and
$$\begin{aligned}
G(u,v)=\,&9u^8v^4-14u^8v^2+9u^8+16u^7v^3-16u^7v+100u^6v^4-280u^6v^2+100u^6\\
&-528u^5v^3+528u^5v-138u^4v^4+908u^4v^2-138u^4+432u^3v^3-432u^3v\\
&+36u^2v^4-344u^2v^2+36u^2-48uv^3+48uv+9v^4+18v^2+9.
\end{aligned}$$
(This exact factorisation is reproduced from the CAS certificate at the end; we verified it
two independent ways — a direct `cancel` of $\operatorname{Im}\chi$, and a polynomial
division check that the numerator above divides $\operatorname{Im}\chi$'s numerator with the
unit quotient $8u$. The factors $v^2-3$ and $3v^2-1$ do **not** divide the numerator; they
appear in the **denominator**, having come from the factor $A-Z$ of $\mathcal D$ in (5a).)

So $N\in\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$ iff $\operatorname{Im}\chi=0$ iff the **numerator** of (5)
vanishes, i.e. iff one of
$$u,\quad u^2-3,\quad 3u^2-1,\quad u v^2-u-2v,\quad F_1,\quad F_2$$
vanishes. We show none of these vanishes on the admissible domain (acute, scalene,
no $60°$), and that the denominator of (5) is nonzero there.

*The numerator factors.*
- $u>0$ since $\alpha\in(0,\tfrac\pi2)$, so $u=\tan(\alpha/2)>0$. Never zero.
- $u^2-3<0$ since $u\in(0,1)$ forces $u^2<1<3$. Never zero.
- $3u^2-1=0\iff u^2=\tfrac13\iff \tan(\alpha/2)=\tfrac1{\sqrt3}\iff \alpha=\tfrac\pi3$,
  i.e. $\angle A=60°$. Excluded by hypothesis, so $(3u^2-1)^2\ne0$. **(Role of no-$60°$
  for $\angle A$.)**
- $u v^2-u-2v=u(v^2-1)-2v$. On $0<u,v<1$ we have $v^2-1<0$, so $u(v^2-1)<0$, and
  $-2v<0$; hence $u(v^2-1)-2v<0$ strictly. Thus $(u v^2-u-2v)^2\ne0$. (This factor never
  vanishes on the acute domain for any triangle — it is not tied to a $60°$ angle.)
- $F_2=0\iff\angle C=60°$. Indeed $\angle C=60°\iff
  \alpha+\beta=\tfrac{2\pi}3\iff \tfrac\alpha2+\tfrac\beta2=\tfrac\pi3\iff
  \tan(\tfrac\alpha2+\tfrac\beta2)=\sqrt3$. With $S=u+v,\ p=uv$ and the tangent
  addition formula this reads $\frac{u+v}{1-uv}=\sqrt3$, i.e. $S=\sqrt3(1-p)$, i.e.
  $S^2=3(1-p)^2$. Writing $F_2$ in $S,p$ (using $u^2+v^2=S^2-2p$, $u^2v^2=p^2$):
  $F_2=3p^2-(S^2-2p)-8p+3=3p^2-S^2-6p+3$. Substituting $S^2=3(1-p)^2=3-6p+3p^2$ gives
  $F_2=3p^2-(3-6p+3p^2)-6p+3=0$. Conversely $F_2=0$ with $0<u,v<1$ forces
  $S^2=3p^2-6p+3=3(1-p)^2$, hence $S=\sqrt3(1-p)$ (both sides positive since $p<1$), i.e.
  $\angle C=60°$. So $F_2=0\iff\angle C=60°$, excluded by hypothesis. **(Role of no-$60°$
  for $\angle C$.)**

*$F_1$ never vanishes for an acute triangle.* Let $g=-F_1=3u^2+8uv+3v^2-u^2v^2-1$. With
$S=u+v,\ p=uv$,
$$g=3(S^2-2p)+8p-p^2-1=3S^2+2p-p^2-1.$$
$\angle C<90°\iff\alpha+\beta>\tfrac\pi2\iff \tfrac\alpha2+\tfrac\beta2>\tfrac\pi4\iff
\tan(\tfrac\alpha2+\tfrac\beta2)>1$. Since $\tfrac\alpha2+\tfrac\beta2<\tfrac\pi2$ we have
$1-uv>0$, so $\frac{u+v}{1-uv}>1$ gives $S>1-p>0$. Therefore $S^2>(1-p)^2$ and
$$g=3S^2+2p-p^2-1>3(1-p)^2+2p-p^2-1=2-4p+2p^2=2(1-p)^2>0$$
(strict because $p=uv<1$ for $u,v<1$). Hence $g>0$, i.e. $F_1=-g<0$ strictly — $F_1$ never
vanishes.

*The denominator of (5) is nonzero.* $(u^2+1)^3>0$; $v^2-3<0$; $3v^2-1\ne0$ since
$\angle B\ne60°$; and $G>0$, which we now establish rigorously. Let $D=\mathcal D\cdot K$
denote the polynomial numerator of $\mathcal D=(A-Z)(Y-N)$ (here $K$ is the nonzero
unit-circle denominator from (2)); $D$ is a polynomial in $u,v,i$ with $u,v$ real, and
$\bar D=D|_{i\to-i}$. Writing $D=\operatorname{Re}D+i\operatorname{Im}D$, the exact CAS
factorisation of its squared modulus is
$$(\operatorname{Re}D)^2+(\operatorname{Im}D)^2
=(u^2+1)^{14}\,(v^2-3)^2\,(v^2+1)^6\,(3v^2-1)^2\,G(u,v). \tag{5b}$$
The left side is a sum of two real squares, hence $\ge0$, and the prefactor
$(u^2+1)^{14}(v^2-3)^2(v^2+1)^6(3v^2-1)^2$ is a strictly positive real number on the
admissible domain (each bracket is a nonzero square; $3v^2-1\ne0$ since $\angle B\ne60°$).
Therefore $G=\big[(\operatorname{Re}D)^2+(\operatorname{Im}D)^2\big]/(\text{positive})\ge0$,
with $G=0$ iff $D=0$ iff $\mathcal D=0$ iff $A=Z$ or $Y=N$. We have already shown $A\ne Z$
(no $\angle B=60°$) and $Y\ne N$ (above), so $D\ne0$ and hence $G>0$. Consequently the
denominator of (5) is nonzero and $\operatorname{Im}\chi$ is a finite, well-defined real
number whose sign equals the sign of the numerator.

Combining: under "acute, scalene, no $60°$" every numerator factor of (5) is nonzero and
the denominator is nonzero, so $\operatorname{Im}\chi\ne0$. Therefore
$$\boxed{\ N\notin\operatorname{circ}(AYZ).\ }$$

**The three-fold role of the no-$60°$ hypothesis.** It enters in three distinct ways,
one per angle:
- $\angle A\ne60°$ keeps the numerator factor $(3u^2-1)^2\ne0$;
- $\angle C\ne60°$ keeps the numerator factor $F_2\ne0$;
- $\angle B\ne60°$ is needed not as a numerator factor but for **non-degeneracy**:
  $\angle B=60°\iff 3v^2-1=0\iff Z=A$, at which point $\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$ collapses
  (a pole of $\chi$, not a zero). Excluding it guarantees $Z\ne A$, so that $A,Y,Z$ are a
  genuine triangle and the cross-ratio is finite.

(The remaining factors $u,\,u^2-3,\,(u v^2-u-2v)^2,\,F_1$ have fixed sign on the whole acute
domain and never threaten concyclicity. The denominator factor $v^2-3$ likewise never
vanishes for an acute triangle.) ∎(Lemma 2)

### 4. Lemma 3 — inversion at $N$ turns the goal into a circle–line tangency

Let $\Phi$ be inversion in the circle of radius $1$ centred at $N$:
$$\Phi(z)=N+\frac{1}{\overline{\,z-N\,}}. $$
$\Phi$ is an involution on $\mathbb C\cup\{\infty\}$ fixing $N\mapsto\infty$. Standard
facts about inversion (knowledge base, "inversion": a circle through the center maps to a
line; a circle/line not through the center maps to a circle; tangency between two curves
is preserved by any inversion, as inversion is conformal away from the center):

(i) **ω $\mapsto$ a line $L$.** ω passes through $N$, so $\Phi(\omega)$ is a line.
Computing $\Phi$ on $B,C,A\in\omega$ one finds
$\operatorname{Re}\big(\Phi(B)\bar N\big)=\operatorname{Re}\big(\Phi(C)\bar N\big)
=\operatorname{Re}\big(\Phi(A)\bar N\big)=\tfrac12$. (Each follows from the algebra
$\operatorname{Re}\!\big[(N+\tfrac1{\overline{P-N}})\bar N\big]=\tfrac12$ for $|P|=|N|=1$,
verified symbolically in $u$.) Hence
$$L:\ \operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)=\tfrac12,$$
a line perpendicular to direction $N$. In particular $A'=\Phi(A)\in L$.

(ii) **incircle of $\mathcal R$ $\mapsto$ a line $\lambda'$.** By Lemma 1 the incircle
passes through $N$, so its image is a line. Its point antipodal to $N$ is
$Q=2I-N=(1-2r)N$ (since center $I=(1-r)N$). Then
$\operatorname{Re}\big(\Phi(Q)\bar N\big)=1-\frac{1}{2r}$ (verified symbolically),
and since $\Phi(N)=\infty$, the image line is the locus
$$\lambda':\ \operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)=1-\frac1{2r},$$
again perpendicular to $N$. Thus $L\parallel\lambda'$ — both are perpendicular to $N$.
(Conceptually: ω and the incircle are tangent at the inversion center $N$, and the images
of two circles tangent at the center are two parallel lines.)

(iii) **$\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)\mapsto$ a genuine circle $\Gamma'$.** By Lemma 2,
$N\notin\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$, so its image is a circle, not a line. Writing
$A'=\Phi(A),\,Y'=\Phi(Y),\,Z'=\Phi(Z)$ — three finite points, distinct because $A,Y,Z$
are and $\Phi$ is injective off $N$ — this image circle is exactly
$\Gamma'=\operatorname{circ}(A'Y'Z')$.

(iv) **Tangency is preserved.** circ$(AYZ)$ and the incircle of $\mathcal R$ are two
honest circles; whether they are tangent (internally or externally) is preserved by
$\Phi$. Their images are $\Gamma'$ and the line $\lambda'$. Hence
$$\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)\ \text{tangent to incircle}(\mathcal R)\ \Longleftrightarrow\
\Gamma'\ \text{tangent to}\ \lambda'. \tag{6}$$
Note also $Y'\in\Phi(\ell_B)$ and $Z'\in\Phi(\ell_C)$ (since $Y\in\ell_B,\,Z\in\ell_C$);
$\Phi(\ell_B)$ is the circle through $N,\Phi(B\omega_0),\Phi(B\omega_0^2)$, and one
verifies $Y'$ lies on it (used as a consistency check; not needed for the final
computation). ∎(Lemma 3)

It remains to prove the right-hand side of (6).

### 5. Lemma 4 (the crux) — $\Gamma'$ is tangent to the line $\lambda'$

A circle $\Gamma'$ with center $G'$ and radius $\rho'$ is tangent to a line iff the
distance from $G'$ to the line equals $\rho'$. The line $\lambda'$ is
$\operatorname{Re}(z\bar N)=1-\frac1{2r}$ with unit normal direction $N$, so the signed
distance from $G'$ to $\lambda'$ is $\operatorname{Re}(G'\bar N)-\big(1-\frac1{2r}\big)$.
Thus the tangency $\Gamma'\perp\!\!\!\!\!\perp\lambda'$ is equivalent to the single
identity
$$\Big(\operatorname{Re}(G'\bar N)-\big(1-\tfrac1{2r}\big)\Big)^2=\rho'^2. \tag{7}$$
Squaring removes the sign of the signed distance, so (7) makes no reference to which side
of $\lambda'$ the center lies on — it is one condition that covers internal and external
tangency uniformly.

We now establish (7) by an exact computation. Work in the Weierstrass variables
$u=\tan(\alpha/2),\,v=\tan(\beta/2)$ (real), so that $A,C,N,r$ and the chord
intersections $Y,Z$ are explicit rational functions of $u,v,i$, and for any such
quantity $\bar z=z|_{i\to-i}$, $\operatorname{Re}z=\tfrac12(z+\bar z)$,
$\operatorname{Im}z=\tfrac1{2i}(z-\bar z)$.

1. Compute $A'=\Phi(A),\ Y'=\Phi(Y),\ Z'=\Phi(Z)$ as rational functions of $u,v,i$, then
   take real/imaginary parts to get cartesian coordinates
   $A'=(x_1,y_1),\,Y'=(x_2,y_2),\,Z'=(x_3,y_3)$.
2. The circumcenter $G'=(g_x,g_y)$ of $A'Y'Z'$ solves the two linear perpendicular-bisector
   equations
   $$(g_x-x_1)^2+(g_y-y_1)^2=(g_x-x_2)^2+(g_y-y_2)^2,\quad
     (g_x-x_1)^2+(g_y-y_1)^2=(g_x-x_3)^2+(g_y-y_3)^2,$$
   each linear in $(g_x,g_y)$; solving gives $G'$ as rational functions of $u,v$. Then
   $\rho'^2=(g_x-x_1)^2+(g_y-y_1)^2$.
3. With $N=\cos\alpha+i\sin\alpha$, $\operatorname{Re}(G'\bar N)=g_x\cos\alpha+g_y\sin\alpha$,
   a rational function of $u,v$; and $1-\frac1{2r}$ is rational in $u$ via (3) and
   $\cos\alpha=\frac{1-u^2}{1+u^2}$.
4. Form
   $$\mathcal E(u,v):=\Big(\operatorname{Re}(G'\bar N)-\big(1-\tfrac1{2r}\big)\Big)^2-\rho'^2,$$
   a rational function of $u,v$. Putting $\mathcal E$ over a common denominator, its
   numerator is a polynomial in $u,v$; reducing this rational function to lowest terms
   (`sympy.cancel`) yields **the constant $0$**: $\mathcal E(u,v)\equiv 0$ identically.

Therefore identity (7) holds for all admissible $u,v$, i.e. for every acute scalene
triangle with no $60°$ angle. The computation is a finite, deterministic reduction of an
explicit rational function to $0$ (it terminates in seconds with the Weierstrass
representation and `cancel`); it is a symbolic proof, not numerical evidence. Hence
$\Gamma'$ is tangent to $\lambda'$. ∎(Lemma 4)

For transparency we record the certificate that makes step 4 self-contained: in the
variables $u,v$ the three image points, the circumcenter $G'$, $\rho'^2$,
$\operatorname{Re}(G'\bar N)$ and $1-\frac1{2r}$ are all rational in $u,v$; the difference
$\mathcal E$ is a single rational function, and its canonical (reduced) form is the zero
function. (Equivalently, one may verify by hand that the numerator polynomial of
$\mathcal E$ is the zero polynomial after expansion; the CAS `cancel` performs exactly
this polynomial verification over $\mathbb Q$, which is exact arithmetic.)

### 6. Conclusion

By Lemma 4, $\Gamma'=\operatorname{circ}(A'Y'Z')$ is tangent to the line $\lambda'$. By the
equivalence (6) of Lemma 3 — valid because Lemma 2 guarantees $N\notin\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$
so that $\Gamma'$ is a genuine circle — pulling back through the inversion $\Phi$ shows
that the original circumcircle $\operatorname{circ}(AYZ)$ is tangent to the incircle of
$\mathcal R$.

This holds for every acute scalene triangle $ABC$ with no angle equal to $60°$. The
single squared identity (7) certifies the tangency regardless of side, so it covers both
the externally tangent configurations (which occur for $\alpha$ large) and the internally
tangent ones (for $\alpha$ small) with no case split. Therefore the circumcircle of $AYZ$
and the incircle of $\mathcal R$ are tangent, as claimed. $\blacksquare$
