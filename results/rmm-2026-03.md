## rmm-2026-03

### Problem restatement
Let $\mathcal{S}$ be a **finite** subset of $\mathbb{R}^3$. Prove that there exist
polynomials $P(x,y,z), Q(x,y,z), R(x,y,z)$ with real coefficients such that a
triple $(a,b,c)$ is in $\mathcal{S}$ **if and only if** the system
$P=a,\ Q=b,\ R=c$ has **no** real solution $(x,y,z)$.

- Domain: algebra (real polynomial maps). Task: proof_only, answer_type: none, difficulty 8.
- Source: RMM 2026, Problem 3.

### Core reformulation (SETTLED, verified-correct)
Writing $F=(P,Q,R):\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^3$, the condition "$P=a,Q=b,R=c$ has
no real solution" is exactly "$(a,b,c)\notin\mathrm{image}(F)$". So the problem is
**equivalent** to:

> Construct a polynomial map $F:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^3$ with
> $\mathrm{image}(F) = \mathbb{R}^3\setminus\mathcal{S}$.

That is, $F$ must miss **exactly** the $n=|\mathcal{S}|$ points of $\mathcal{S}$
and hit every other point. This reformulation is unconditional and is the agreed
starting point for all work below.

## Approaches tried

- **Reformulation to "image $=\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\mathcal{S}$"** — WORKS. Sound
  and used by all subsequent work. Reduces the iff-statement to a single
  image-control problem.

- **Cubic infeasibility certificate + master equivalence** (the spine) — WORKS as
  a *predicate*; see Current best. Gives a verified-correct characterization of
  *which* targets must be hit, but does not by itself yield $P,Q,R$.

- **Positive-range gate $e(u,v)=(uv-1)^2+u^2$** — WORKS as a building block; image
  $=(0,\infty)$ (verified). Shows a polynomial image can be non-closed (omit
  boundary/isolated values). Necessary ingredient, not sufficient.

- **Triangular maps** $F=(x,\,y,\,h(x,y,z))$ — DEAD END. Fixing $x=a_i,y=b_i$
  reduces $R=c_i$ to a univariate polynomial in $z$; its range is an interval
  (odd degree $\Rightarrow$ all of $\mathbb{R}$; even degree $\Rightarrow$ a
  half-line $[\min,\infty)$). A univariate range can never be $\mathbb{R}$ minus a
  finite **nonempty** set. So a graph-over-$(x,y)$ map cannot puncture a single
  point — it always misses a whole interval or nothing.

- **Coordinate-wise product maps** $(A(x),B(y),C(z))$ — DEAD END. Image is the
  product set $\mathrm{Im}(A)\times\mathrm{Im}(B)\times\mathrm{Im}(C)$.
  $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\mathcal{S}$ is not a product set for nonempty
  $\mathcal{S}$.

- **Complex polynomial $z\mapsto z^k$ viewed as $\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$** —
  DEAD END. By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra every nonconstant complex
  polynomial is surjective $\mathbb{C}\to\mathbb{C}$, so the associated real map is
  surjective onto all of $\mathbb{R}^2$ (it hits $0$ too). Misses nothing.

- **Single discriminant cutting out the missed set** (reduce solvability to
  "$\mathrm{disc}(a,b,c)\ge 0$") — DEAD END. $\{\mathrm{disc}<0\}$ is an **open**
  set, so this construction can only miss an open set, never a finite nonempty
  one. The discreteness of the missed set must come from a root-existence
  (codimension) condition, not a strict inequality. This is precisely why the
  cubic certificate (root-existence) replaces the discriminant (inequality).

- **"Radius $\times$ direction" / polynomial $\mathbb{R}^2\to S^2$ (or $S^1$)** —
  DEAD END. No polynomial map $\mathbb{R}^2\to S^k$ is surjective (topological
  obstruction: $\mathbb{R}^2$ is contractible / a polynomial map to a sphere is
  not onto). So one cannot factor "miss a point" as "control radius, sweep all
  directions." This removes the most natural bridge from the 1-D positive-range
  gate to a punctured plane.

## Current best

The **reduction layer is verified-correct and worth banking**; the construction
crux is open. The three lemmas below are airtight.

### Lemma 1 (Cubic certificate — VERIFIED, the spine)
For every real $D$, the univariate equation
$$D\,t^3 + t^2 + 1 = 0$$
has a real root in $t$ **iff $D\neq 0$**.

*Proof.* If $D=0$ the equation is $t^2+1=0$, which has no real root. If $D\neq 0$
it is a genuine cubic (odd degree): as $t\to+\infty$ and $t\to-\infty$ the
expression $Dt^3+t^2+1$ tends to $+\infty$ and $-\infty$ in opposite orders
(governed by the sign of $D$), so by the Intermediate Value Theorem it has at
least one real root. $\square$

### Master equivalence (VERIFIED)
Let $\mathcal{S}=\{s_1,\dots,s_n\}\subset\mathbb{R}^3$ and define the
**distance product**
$$d(a,b,c) := \prod_{i=1}^{n}\bigl\lVert (a,b,c)-s_i\bigr\rVert^2
   = \prod_{i=1}^{n}\bigl((a-a_i)^2+(b-b_i)^2+(c-c_i)^2\bigr).$$
Then $d$ is a polynomial, $d\ge 0$ everywhere, and $d$ **vanishes exactly on
$\mathcal{S}$**. Combining with Lemma 1:
$$\exists\,t\in\mathbb{R}\ \text{with}\ d(a,b,c)\,t^3+t^2+1=0
\iff d(a,b,c)\neq 0 \iff (a,b,c)\notin\mathcal{S}.$$
This pins down *exactly which targets should be hit*: a target is "hit" iff this
cubic-in-$t$ has a real root. At each $s_i\in\mathcal{S}$ the cubic degenerates to
$t^2+1>0$, which is the explicit (Real-Nullstellensatz-style) positivity witness of
infeasibility.

### Lemma 2 (Positive-range gate — VERIFIED)
$e(u,v):=(uv-1)^2+u^2$ has image exactly the open ray $(0,\infty)$: it is never
$0$ (if $u=0$ then $e=1$; if $u\neq 0$ both squares cannot vanish since
$uv-1=0\Rightarrow u\neq0$); its infimum is $0$, approached but not attained along
$u\to 0,\ v=1/u$; and it is unbounded above. So a polynomial image can be a
non-closed subset of $\mathbb{R}$ — the mechanism by which an image can omit an
isolated/boundary value.

### Coordinate normalization (sound, not the blocker)
A generic linear automorphism $\Phi$ of $\mathbb{R}^3$ (a polynomial map with
polynomial — indeed linear — inverse) can be applied so that the points of
$\Phi(\mathcal{S})$ have pairwise distinct first coordinates and pairwise distinct
$(a,b)$-projections; bad choices lie in finitely many hyperplanes of coefficient
space. Since $\mathrm{image}(\Phi^{-1}\circ F)=\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\mathcal{S}
\iff \mathrm{image}(F)=\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\Phi(\mathcal{S})$, it suffices to
solve the normalized problem. This step is fine; it is *not* the gap.

### Base case $n=0$ (settled)
If $\mathcal{S}=\emptyset$, take $F=\mathrm{id}$ (so $P=x,Q=y,R=z$); the image is
all of $\mathbb{R}^3$ and the system is solvable for every target. Done.

## The precise OPEN crux

Everything above is bookkeeping around a single unconstructed step.

**The crux.** Produce an *explicit* polynomial map $\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$
(equivalently, for $n=1$, an explicit $F:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^3$) whose image
is **the plane (resp. space) minus exactly one point**, together with a proof of
both inclusions. Parametrizing such a map over a "level" coordinate $x$ (gated by
$g(x)=\prod_i(x-a_i)$, which vanishes at the bad levels) and gluing the $n$
punctures is the easy bookkeeping *once one punctured map exists*; even the $n=1$
sub-case is unsolved precisely because this map is missing.

**Why the cubic certificate does not close it.** Lemma 1 + the master equivalence
yield a **predicate** on the *output* $(a,b,c)$: "this target should be hit iff
$\exists t:\ d(a,b,c)t^3+t^2+1=0$." But $d$ depends on $(a,b,c)$ — the very thing
the map must *produce*. The certificate is a *test that a given target satisfies*,
not a *parametrization that generates* the targets. Turning the predicate into
polynomials $P,Q,R$ requires writing them as functions of the **source**
variables and proving image$=\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\mathcal S$ directly; the
certificate does not survive into that construction (it only tells us which point
must be omitted).

**The structural obstruction (why naive gadgets fail).** The construction must
break a circular dependence: if the third output is a graph over $(a,b)$
(triangular map), its fiberwise range is an interval and can never be "line minus
a point" (recorded dead end). So $b$ and $c$ must *both* move with the source, so
the fiber is not a graph over $(a,b)$. The simplest planar gadgets in this family
miss a **line minus a point** or a **ray**, never a single point:
e.g. $(s,t)\mapsto(s,\,st-1)$ has image $\{b\neq 0\}\cup\{(0,-1)\}$ (a line minus a
point removed wrong), and Lemma 2's gate misses a *ray* in $\mathbb{R}^1$. Lifting
"miss a ray/line" to "miss exactly one point of $\mathbb{R}^2$" is the genuinely
hard, still-open step. The natural bridge ("radius $\times$ direction") is killed by
the $\mathbb{R}^2\to S^1$ non-surjectivity dead end, and the outline supplied no
replacement.
