## Current best
Complete proof below. The maximum game length for two decks of `n` cards is

## Full proof

Throughout, fix `n` cards per deck (the problem is the case `n=40`). One **move** is one
minute. We prove the maximum length of a game is

    T(n) = n(n^2+2)/3   minutes,

and then evaluate at `n=40`.

### Setup, notation, and the determinism hypothesis

A configuration of the system is an ordered pair `(A, B)` where `A` is the current deck `A`
listed from top to bottom and likewise `B`; both lists contain the same number of cards.
A move looks at the two top cards `a` (of `A`) and `b` (of `B`) and applies the **duel
outcome** of the unordered identity pair `{a,b}`, which by hypothesis is a fixed function
of the two cards, independent of all history. For an ordered top pair `(a,b)` we name the
four mutually exclusive outcomes:

- **A** ("a wins"): `b` goes to the bottom of `B`; `A` is unchanged (`a` stays on top).
- **B** ("b wins"): `a` goes to the bottom of `A`; `B` is unchanged (`b` stays on top).
- **T** ("tie"): both `a` and `b` are removed from their decks.
- **N** ("no interaction"): `a` goes to the bottom of `A` and `b` goes to the bottom of `B`.

Because the outcome of a configuration's top pair is uniquely determined, **every
configuration has a unique successor**: the dynamics is a deterministic function on the
(finite) set of configurations.

A **game** is, by definition, a process that **terminates** (both decks become empty). We
must bound the length over all games. We will use termination as a hypothesis where noted.

A **phase** is a maximal run of moves between two consecutive ties (equivalently, between
the start, or a tie, and the next tie). Within a phase the two decks contain a fixed set of
cards (no card is added or removed; A/B/N moves only permute a deck). Say a phase has `k`
cards in each deck.

### Part A. The cyclic-rotation invariant

> **Lemma 1 (rotation invariant).** Consider a phase whose decks at the start of the phase
> are listed (top to bottom) as `A = [a^0, a^1, ..., a^{k-1}]` and `B = [b^0, ..., b^{k-1}]`.
> Then at every moment of the phase, deck `A` equals a cyclic rotation of `[a^0,...,a^{k-1}]`
> and deck `B` equals a cyclic rotation of `[b^0,...,b^{k-1}]`. Consequently the
> configuration is determined by a pair `(r,s) ∈ Z_k × Z_k`, where the top of `A` is
> `a^{r}` (indices mod `k`) and the top of `B` is `b^{s}`.

*Proof.* A single move within the phase is one of A, B, N (a T ends the phase). We check
each preserves "each deck is a cyclic rotation of its phase-start order," by examining its
effect on each deck:

- **A (a wins).** Deck `A` is unchanged, so it stays the same rotation of `[a^i]`. Deck `B`:
  its top card `b` is moved to the bottom of `B`; moving the top card of a list to the
  bottom is precisely a cyclic rotation by `1`. So if `B` was the rotation taking `b^s` to
  the top, it becomes the rotation taking `b^{s+1}` to the top. Both decks remain cyclic
  rotations of their phase-start orders.
- **B (b wins).** Symmetric to A with the roles of the decks swapped: `B` is unchanged, and
  `A`'s top card goes to the bottom, a cyclic rotation by `1`.
- **N (no interaction).** Both top cards go to the bottom of their respective decks; each is
  a cyclic rotation by `1`. Both decks remain cyclic rotations of their phase-start orders.

Thus each of the three intra-phase moves maps a (rotation of `[a^i]`, rotation of `[b^j]`)
configuration to another such configuration. Since the phase begins in the trivial
rotation `(r,s)=(0,0)`, induction on the number of moves into the phase proves the
invariant. Encoding "`A` is the rotation whose top is `a^r`" by `r ∈ Z_k` and "`B` is the
rotation whose top is `b^s`" by `s ∈ Z_k`, the configuration is exactly the pair `(r,s)`,
and the top identity pair is `(a^r, b^s)`. ∎

Under this encoding the three intra-phase moves act on `(r,s) ∈ Z_k × Z_k` as:

    A: (r,s) -> (r, s+1)      (B rotates by 1)
    B: (r,s) -> (r+1, s)      (A rotates by 1)
    N: (r,s) -> (r+1, s+1)    (both rotate by 1)

and a **T** at `(r,s)` ends the phase (removing `a^r` and `b^s`). Because the outcome is a
fixed function of the identity pair `(a^r,b^s)`, and `(a^r,b^s)` is a fixed function of
`(r,s)`, the outcome is a fixed function of `(r,s)`; in particular each `(r,s)` has a unique
successor (out-degree `1`).

### Part B. Upper bound: `T(n) ≤ n(n^2+2)/3`

> **Lemma 2 (no repeated configuration).** In a game (a terminating process) no
> configuration occurs twice.

*Proof.* The dynamics is deterministic: each configuration has a unique successor. If a
configuration `C` occurred at two distinct times `t_1 < t_2`, then determinism forces the
entire trajectory to repeat the block from `t_1` to `t_2` forever, so the process never
terminates. This contradicts the assumption that the process is a game. Hence no
configuration repeats. ∎

In particular, **within a single phase** (where, by Lemma 1, configurations correspond
bijectively to states `(r,s) ∈ Z_k × Z_k`), no state `(r,s)` is visited twice.

> **Lemma 3 (present-partner / global tie bijection).** In a game, the ties induce a
> bijection `f : {cards of A} -> {cards of B}` (each card of `A` is removed by tying exactly
> one card of `B`, and distinct cards tie distinct partners). Moreover, in any phase, if
> `S_A`, `S_B` are the sets of cards present in `A`, `B`, then `f` restricts to a bijection
> `S_A -> S_B`.

*Proof.* A card leaves its deck only at a tie (the moves A, B, N never remove a card). Since
the game terminates, every card of `A` eventually leaves, hence eventually ties some card of
`B`; and since a tie removes both partners simultaneously and a removed card never returns, a
card of `A` ties exactly one card of `B` (the one with which it tied at the unique moment of
its removal). Each tie removes one card from each deck, so over the whole game the `n` ties
pair up the `n` cards of `A` with the `n` cards of `B`; thus `f` is a well-defined bijection.

Now fix a phase with present sets `S_A, S_B`. Take any `a ∈ S_A`. Card `a` is still in the
game, so by the previous paragraph it will eventually be removed, and its removal is a tie
with `f(a)`. A tie removes both partners at once, and removed cards never return; therefore
at the time `a` is removed, `f(a)` is also still present, and in fact `f(a)` must still be
present *now* (it can only leave the game at the very moment `a` leaves, which has not yet
happened). Hence `f(a) ∈ S_B`. So `f(S_A) ⊆ S_B`. As `f` is injective and `|S_A| = |S_B| = k`
(each tie removes one card from each deck, keeping the deck sizes equal), `f(S_A) = S_B`, and
`f` restricts to a bijection `S_A -> S_B`. ∎

> **Lemma 4 (phase budget).** A phase with `k` cards per deck has length at most
> `k(k-1)+1` moves.

*Proof.* Index the present cards of `A` in phase-start order as `a^0,...,a^{k-1}` and of `B`
as `b^0,...,b^{k-1}`, so configurations are states `(r,s) ∈ Z_k × Z_k` (Lemma 1). Call
`(r,s)` a **tie state** if the top pair `(a^r, b^s)` is a tie pair, and a **non-tie state**
otherwise.

By Lemma 3, the global bijection `f` restricts to a bijection of the present sets
`S_A = {a^0,...,a^{k-1}} -> S_B = {b^0,...,b^{k-1}}`. For each row `r ∈ Z_k`, the card `a^r`
is present and its tie partner `f(a^r)` is present, so `f(a^r) = b^{s}` for a unique
`s ∈ Z_k`; the pair `(a^r, b^s)` ties, so `(r,s)` is a tie state. Distinct rows `r ≠ r'` give
distinct present cards `a^r ≠ a^{r'}`, hence (by injectivity of `f`) distinct partners and
distinct tie states `(r,s) ≠ (r',s')` with `r ≠ r'`. Therefore there are **at least `k`
distinct tie states** (one in each row), and consequently at most

    k^2 - k = k(k-1)

**non-tie states** among the `k^2` states.

The phase is a walk on the state graph that, by determinism, takes a non-tie move (A, B, or
N) at every non-tie state and exits the phase at the first tie state it reaches. By Lemma 2,
no state is visited twice, so the walk visits at most `k(k-1)` distinct non-tie states before
the exit; each visit to a non-tie state is one move, and the exit (the tie) is one more move.
Hence the phase has at most `k(k-1)` non-tie moves plus `1` tie move, i.e.

    length of phase ≤ k(k-1) + 1.

(If the phase never reaches a tie it would run forever, contradicting termination; so a tie
exit exists, and the count above is finite.) ∎

> **Lemma 5 (recurrence and closed form for the upper bound).** Let `L(n)` be the maximum
> length of a game on two decks of `n` cards. Then `L(n) ≤ U(n)` where `U(1)=1` and
> `U(n) = U(n-1) + n(n-1) + 1`. Solving, `U(n) = n(n^2+2)/3`.

*Proof.* Take any game on `n` cards. By Lemma 4 (with `k=n`) its first phase has at most
`n(n-1)+1` moves and ends with a tie that removes one card from each deck, leaving decks of
`n-1` cards. By Lemma 1, after the first tie both decks are rotations of the surviving
`n-1` cards (removing the two tied cards from rotations of the `n`-card lists leaves the
remaining cards in cyclically consistent order in each deck); regardless, the remainder of
the process is itself a game on two decks of `n-1` cards (same deterministic outcome rule
restricted to the surviving cards, terminating because the whole game does). Its length is at
most `L(n-1)`. Therefore

    (length of game) ≤ [n(n-1) + 1] + L(n-1),

and taking the max over all games, `L(n) ≤ n(n-1) + 1 + L(n-1)`. The base case `n=1`: one
deck-card each, the single pair must tie (it is the only way to empty the deck, since the
game terminates), so `L(1) = 1`. Hence `L(n) ≤ U(n)` with the stated recurrence.

Now solve `U(n) = U(1) + Σ_{k=2}^{n} [k(k-1)+1]`. Since `U(1)=1 = 1·0+1`, we may include
`k=1` and write `U(n) = Σ_{k=1}^{n} [k(k-1)+1]`. Compute the two sums:

    Σ_{k=1}^{n} 1 = n,
    Σ_{k=1}^{n} k(k-1) = Σ_{k=1}^{n} (k^2 - k)
                       = [n(n+1)(2n+1)/6] - [n(n+1)/2]
                       = n(n+1)[(2n+1) - 3]/6
                       = n(n+1)(2n-2)/6
                       = (n-1)n(n+1)/3.

(The identity `Σ_{k=1}^{n}k^2 = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6` and `Σ_{k=1}^{n}k = n(n+1)/2` are the
standard power-sum formulas; here `Σ k(k-1) = (n-1)n(n+1)/3` also follows from the
hockey-stick identity `Σ_{k=1}^{n} 2·C(k,2) = 2·C(n+1,3)`.) Therefore

    U(n) = n + (n-1)n(n+1)/3 = [3n + (n-1)n(n+1)]/3.

Expand `(n-1)n(n+1) = n(n^2-1) = n^3 - n`, so

    U(n) = (3n + n^3 - n)/3 = (n^3 + 2n)/3 = n(n^2+2)/3.

This proves `L(n) ≤ n(n^2+2)/3 =: T(n)`. ∎

### Part C. Lower bound: an explicit game of length exactly `T(n)`

We exhibit a fixed outcome table on `n`-card decks whose (unique) game lasts exactly `T(n)`
moves; combined with Part B this shows `L(n) = T(n)`.

**The construction.** Label the cards of `A` by `0, 1, ..., n-1` in initial top-to-bottom
order, and likewise the cards of `B` by `0, 1, ..., n-1`. Define the outcome of an ordered
top pair `(a,b)` (where `a ∈ A`, `b ∈ B`) purely from the labels via `s := a + b` (ordinary
integer sum, with `0 ≤ a,b ≤ n-1`):

    outcome(a,b) =  T   if s = n-1,
                    A   if s ≥ n,
                    N   if s = n-2,
                    A   if s < n-2.

This depends only on the unordered identity pair `{a,b}` through `s=a+b`, so it is a valid
fixed outcome table (in particular the outcome **B** never occurs in this construction;
that is allowed). The table is fixed **once and for all** on the original `n^2` identity
pairs; we never redefine an outcome mid-game. We claim the resulting game terminates and has
length exactly `T(n)`.

> **Lemma 6 (length of the construction).** Let `Lc(n)` be the length of the game produced
> by the table above on `n`-card decks. Then `Lc(n) = T(n) = n(n^2+2)/3` for all `n ≥ 1`.

*Proof.* We prove two facts and combine them by induction on `n`:

  (i) **First-phase structure.** The first phase lasts exactly `n(n-1)+1` moves and ends in
      the configuration `A = [0, 1, ..., n-2]`, `B = [1, 2, ..., n-1]`.

  (ii) **Self-similar reset.** After relabeling `B`'s cards by `b ↦ b-1`, the residual
      configuration of (i) is order-isomorphic to the fresh size-`(n-1)` instance of the
      same construction.

**Base case `n=1`.** Decks are `A=[0]`, `B=[0]`. The top pair is `(0,0)` with `s=0=n-1`, so
the outcome is **T**, removing both cards; the game ends. `Lc(1) = 1 = T(1)` (since
`T(1)=1·3/3=1`). Facts (i)/(ii) are vacuous/trivial here.

**Proof of (i) for `n ≥ 2`.** We track the first phase via the rotation coordinates of
Lemma 1: the phase starts at `A=[0,...,n-1]`, `B=[0,...,n-1]`, so the top of `A` is `a` (its
own label, since the phase-start order is the identity labeling) and the top of `B` is its
current top label `b`. We show the play proceeds card-of-`A` by card-of-`A`:

We claim that while the top card of `A` equals a fixed value `a`, the top card of `B` runs
through `n-1` consecutive labels (each A-win sends the current `B`-top to the bottom,
advancing `B`'s top label by `+1` mod `n`, by Lemma 1), and the run ends as follows:

- For `a = 0`: the `B`-top starts at `b=0` (initial configuration). The successive top pairs
  are `(0,0),(0,1),...,(0,n-2)` with sums `s = 0,1,...,n-2`. By the table, `s=0,...,n-3` give
  **A** (each is `< n-2`, a win, since `n≥2`), and the run reaches `s=n-2` at the pair
  `(0,n-2)`, which is **N**. The **N** move advances *both* tops by `+1` (mod `n`): `A`'s top
  becomes `a=1`, and `B`'s top advances from `n-2` to `n-1`. Note the pair `(0,n-1)` with
  `s=n-1` (the tie of `a=0`) is **skipped** — it is exactly the partner that is deferred.
  This run has `n-1` moves (`b=0,...,n-2`), the last being **N**.

- For `a ∈ {1, ..., n-2}`: when card `a` first reaches the top of `A` (via the **N** move
  that ended card `a-1`'s run), the top of `B` is `b = n-a`. We verify this by an immediate
  induction: card `0`'s run ends with `B`-top advancing to `n-1 = n-0-1`; and the **N**
  ending card `a-1`'s run advances `B`'s top by `+1`. We claim card `a-1`'s run ends with
  `B`-top equal to `n-1-(a-1) = n-a`, hence after the `+1` of the **N** move card `a`'s run
  starts with `B`-top `n-a+1`... let us instead read the start value directly from the
  established end value: it is cleaner to state and prove the invariant

      (∗) card a's run starts with B-top label  b_0(a) = (n-a) mod n  for a ≥ 1, and
          b_0(0) = 0,

  together with: each run ends with an **N** move (for `a ≤ n-2`) or a **T** move (for
  `a = n-1`), and the run consists of exactly the `B`-top labels `b_0(a), b_0(a)+1, ...`
  taken mod `n` up to (but not skipping past) the special value. We now justify (∗) and the
  run length for `a ∈ {1,...,n-2}` directly.

  While `a` is on top, the `B`-top label `b` increases by `1` mod `n` per move (A-win), and:
  the **tie** of `a` would occur at `b = n-1-a` (`s=n-1`), and the **N** of `a` occurs at
  `b = n-2-a` (`s=n-2`). Starting from `b_0(a) = n-a` (we verify `b_0(a)=n-a` below), the
  labels visited are, in order,
  `n-a, n-a+1, ..., n-1, 0, 1, ..., n-2-a` (all taken mod `n`). This list is exactly
  `Z_n \ {n-1-a}`: it starts just after `n-1-a` (namely at `n-a`) and wraps around to end
  just before `n-1-a` (namely at `n-2-a`). Hence the **tie** label `n-1-a` is never the top
  during this run (it is skipped, sitting between the end `n-2-a` and start `n-a`), and the
  run terminates at `b = n-2-a` with the **N** move. The number of labels in
  `Z_n \ {n-1-a}` is `n-1`, so the run has exactly `n-1` moves, the last being **N**.
  After this **N**, both tops advance by `+1`: `A`'s top becomes `a+1`, and `B`'s top
  advances from `n-2-a` to `n-1-a = n-(a+1)`, which is `b_0(a+1)`, confirming the recursion
  `b_0(a+1) = n-(a+1)` and hence (∗) by induction starting from `b_0(1)`:

  For the start `b_0(1)`: card `0`'s run ends (shown above) at the **N** move on pair
  `(0,n-2)`, after which `B`'s top advances from `n-2` to `n-1 = n-1`. Thus `b_0(1)=n-1=n-a`
  at `a=1`. This anchors the induction, and (∗) holds for all `a ∈ {1,...,n-2}`.

- For `a = n-1` (the last card of `A` to reach the top): by (∗) extended one more step, the
  **N** ending card `n-2`'s run advances `B`'s top from `b_0(n-2)+(\text{run length}-1)`...
  more simply, the **N** ending card `n-2`'s run sets `B`'s top to `n-1-(n-2) = 1` (apply the
  end-of-run formula `b_{end}=n-2-a` at `a=n-2` gives `b_{end}=0`, then **N** advances to
  `1`). So card `n-1`'s run starts with `B`-top `b = 1`. While `a=n-1` is on top, `s=a+b`
  with `b=1,2,...`: `s = n, n+1, ...` give **A** (wins), and the labels run
  `1,2,...,n-1,0`. The **tie** occurs at `s=n-1`, i.e. `b=0`, which is reached last (after
  wrapping). So card `n-1`'s run is `b = 1,2,...,n-1,0`, i.e. all `n` labels, ending at
  `b=0` with the **T** move (`s=(n-1)+0=n-1`). This run has exactly `n` moves, the last
  being the phase-ending tie.

Summing the run lengths: cards `a=0,...,n-2` contribute `n-1` moves each (that is `(n-1)`
cards `× (n-1)` moves `= (n-1)^2`), and card `a=n-1` contributes `n` moves. Total first-phase
length:

    (n-1)^2 + n = n^2 - 2n + 1 + n = n^2 - n + 1 = n(n-1) + 1,

exactly the upper-bound budget of Lemma 4. Moreover the first tie is the pair `(n-1, 0)`,
which removes card `n-1` from `A` and card `0` from `B`. We now determine the residual decks.

At the tie move, `A`'s top is `n-1`; by Lemma 1, `A` is a rotation of `[0,1,...,n-1]` with
`n-1` on top, namely `A = [n-1, 0, 1, ..., n-2]`. `B`'s top is `0`; `B` is a rotation of
`[0,1,...,n-1]` with `0` on top, namely `B = [0,1,...,n-1]`. The tie removes the two top
cards `n-1` (from `A`) and `0` (from `B`), leaving

    A = [0, 1, ..., n-2],   B = [1, 2, ..., n-1].

This establishes (i). (For `n=2`: cards `a=0` contributes `1` move, card `a=1` contributes
`2` moves, total `3 = 2·1+1`; residual `A=[0]`, `B=[1]`. Consistent.)

**Proof of (ii).** The residual configuration has `A = [0,...,n-2]` (top to bottom) and
`B = [1,...,n-1]`. Relabel `B`'s cards by `β(b) = b - 1`, an order-preserving bijection
`{1,...,n-1} -> {0,...,n-2}` that sends the residual `B = [1,...,n-1]` to `[0,...,n-2]`,
while leaving `A = [0,...,n-2]` as is. Under this relabeling the residual instance has both
decks listed as `[0,1,...,n-2]`, i.e. the *initial* configuration of the size-`(n-1)` game.

It remains to check the outcome table also matches the size-`(n-1)` construction under this
relabeling. The outcomes in the residual are still given by the original size-`n` table
(determinism: outcomes are never redefined). For a residual top pair with `A`-label `a` and
relabeled `B`-label `b' = b-1` (so `a ∈ {0,...,n-2}`, `b' ∈ {0,...,n-2}`, original
`b = b'+1`), the size-`n` outcome uses `s = a + b = a + b' + 1`:

    size-n  T  ⇔  s = n-1      ⇔  a + b' = n-2 = (n-1) - 1   ⇔  size-(n-1)  T,
    size-n  A  ⇔  s ≥ n        ⇔  a + b' ≥ n-1 = (n-1)        ⇔  size-(n-1)  A,
    size-n  N  ⇔  s = n-2      ⇔  a + b' = n-3 = (n-1) - 2    ⇔  size-(n-1)  N,
    size-n  A  ⇔  s < n-2      ⇔  a + b' < n-3 = (n-1) - 2    ⇔  size-(n-1)  A.

The right-hand conditions are precisely the size-`(n-1)` table evaluated at `(a,b')` with
`s' = a + b'`. Hence for every residual top pair, the original outcome equals the
size-`(n-1)` construction's outcome under the relabeling `b ↦ b-1`. Since this relabeling is
an order isomorphism of both decks (and the moves A/N act identically under relabeling — they
permute deck positions, which the relabeling respects, and **B** never occurs), the residual
process is move-for-move identical to the size-`(n-1)` construction's game. Therefore the
residual lasts exactly `Lc(n-1)` moves and terminates. This establishes (ii).

**Combining.** By (i) the first phase has `n(n-1)+1` moves; by (ii) the remainder is a faithful
copy of the size-`(n-1)` game, of length `Lc(n-1)`. The whole game terminates (the residual
does, by induction). Hence

    Lc(n) = [n(n-1) + 1] + Lc(n-1),   Lc(1) = 1.

This is the *same* recurrence and base case as `U(n)` in Lemma 5, so by the same solution
(Lemma 5's computation), `Lc(n) = n(n^2+2)/3 = T(n)`. ∎

### Conclusion

By Part B (Lemma 5), every game on `n`-card decks has length at most `T(n) = n(n^2+2)/3`. By
Part C (Lemma 6), the explicit construction attains length exactly `T(n)`. Therefore the
maximum game length on `n`-card decks is exactly

    L(n) = n(n^2 + 2)/3.

**Evaluate at `n = 40`** (the problem):

    T(40) = 40·(40^2 + 2)/3 = 40·(1600 + 2)/3 = 40·1602/3.

Since `1602/3 = 534` and `40·534 = 21360`,

    T(40) = 21360 minutes.

Converting to hours: `21360 / 60 = 356`, and indeed `356·60 = 21360`. Hence the maximum time
a game can last is exactly

    21360 minutes = 356 hours.

(As an internal check of Lemma 5's closed form at `n=40` via the sum directly:
`Σ_{k=1}^{40} k(k-1) = (39·40·41)/3 = 63960/3 = 21320`, plus `Σ_{k=1}^{40} 1 = 40`, giving
`21320 + 40 = 21360`, matching `T(40)`.)

Therefore the maximum time a game can last equals **356 hours**. ∎
